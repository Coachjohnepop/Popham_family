YEAR 1676 (10 paras)
============================================================
In 1662, Richard Sawtelle and his wife Elizabeth (Pople) were now living in the Groton settlement, 40 miles west of City of Boston in the Massachusetts Bay Colony’s western frontier.  Richard was appointed the Groton “town clerk” for the first three years after the new town was formed.  Richard Sawtelle was farming on 20 acres, which was formed out of the original Patatawag plantation.  Richard‘s home in the town of Groton was one of five fortified garrison houses to which the neighbors and Richard’s extended family would come for protection against Native American warrior attacks.  Groton was a frontier settlement when the “King Philip’s War” occurred and was attacked and burned on 13 Mar 1676, after which Richard and Elizabeth moved back to the town of Watertown, where Richard was elected a town “selectman” in 1689.
In the “Battle at the Great Swamp Fort” sixty-eight colonial soldiers were killed and a hundred and fifty were wounded.  Twenty-two additional soldiers died of their wounds on the trip back to towns they came from.  Lt. Phineas Upham was carried over the night, after the attack, back to the town of Wickford in the Rhode Island Colony.  Wounded Lt. Upham was next taken back in the withdrawal of Governor Wilson forces from Rhode Island back to Massachusetts.  The march back in winter was extremely difficult.  The soldiers were forced to kill and eat many their own horses in what was known as the “Hungry March”.  The exact date is unknown, but in Oct 1676, Lt. Phineas Upham died of his battle wounds in his home in the town Malden in the Massachusetts Bay Colony.
In 1676, Richard Sawtelle’s home in Groton in the Massachusetts Bay Colony was one of five garrison houses in Groton.
On 13 March 1676, the town of Groton was burned and the inhabitants were all forced leave.  Richard Sawtelle took his family back 30 miles east to the town Watertown near the City of Boston after the attack on remote frontier town of Groton.
On 16 Jul 1677, at the end of the “King Philip’s War”, widower William Taylor married again widow Hannah (Merriam), the widow of Henry Axtell, who had been killed by Native warriors on the Marlborough-Sudbury Road in April, 1676.  Hannah had five children from her first marriage with Henry.
In 1686, Robert Robbins was one of the first to purchase Nashoba native lands that had been taken from the Wampanoag and Narragansett Native tribes at the end of the “King Philip’s War” back ten years earlier in 1676.
In 1668, Lt. Michel Messier and his brother-in-law Jacques le Moyne received the fief (land) of “Cap de la Trinite”.  When they divided the land in 1676, Lt. Michel Messier’s portion was called “Cap Saint Michel”.  Michel added another large parcel of land next to his “Cap Saint Michel” estate in 1678.
On 24 May 1676, Marguerite Messier was born to Lt. Michel Messier and Anne (Lemoine) in Chambly, Canada.
On 7 Jun 1676, Pierre Gaudin sold his property in Lachine, a settlement next to Villa Marie, to Mathurin Thibdeau.  Pierre Gaudin his wife Jeanne (Rousseliere) then moved to French Acadia (Cape Breton Island) to build a mill for Michel La Vallière the French lord of Acadia.  After the mill was built Pierre and Jeanne moved on and settled in Port Royal settlement in Acadia, Canada.
On 31 Aug 1676, thirty-year-old Mathieu Gervais, a discharged French soldier of the “Carignan-Salières Regiment” and fifteen-year-old Michele Picard were married in Ville-Marie settlement in Canada.  Michele had been born in the Ville-Marie back in 1661.  Mathieu was living and farming on 4 acres of land in La Prairie, Canada when he married.  La Prairie was located on the south side of the St. Lawrence River across from the Ville Marie settlement.

YEAR 1677 (6 paras)
============================================================
On 19 Sep 1677, Joshua Lamb of Roxbury purchased half of the famous “Roanoke Island” in the County of Albemarle in the Province of Carolina for £150 from Nicholas Paige of Boston.  Nicholas had earlier purchased the island from of Sr. William Berkeley, the Governor of the Virginia Colony.
See the map on the next page of 1677 New England.  The six of the Praying Villages are identified with white crosses on the map.
On 29 Oct 1675, Pierre Boucher, Lord of Boucherville, who was looking for settlers who would become tax payers to populate his property on Île Jésus island selected Guillaume Labelle and his father-in-law Olivier Charbonneau to care for his seigneurial farm on Île Jésus Island.  The farm was 45 acres of cultivated land, with building, 300 sheaves of dry Bundled wheat, 600 bales of dry baled hay, 4 oxen and a poultry yard.  On 13 Aug 1677, Pierre Boucher signed over the deed of the farm to Guillaume Labelle changing him and his wife Anne (Carbonneau) from a poor sharecropper to successful prosperous landowner.
On 13 Aug 1677, Pierre Boucher signed a deed conceding land ownership to Guillaume Labelle of the land he and his wife Anne (Charbonneau) and their three young children were farming on Île Jésus Island.  Guillaume was the island’s first land owner and his land was assigned lot number 8 near the Pierre Boucher’s manor house.  Clearly Pierre Boucher like Guillaume and wanted him and his family to be his neighbor.
In 1677, sixty-five-year-old François Bélanger and his wife fifty-three-year-old Marie (Guyon) had 12 children.  Ten of children were either on their own or in the process of leaving the family home.  François accepted a concession of land and a position as seigneur of Bonsecours, which would later become known as “L'Islet”.  The property was located downstream from Île d'Orléans.  Taking such a step was often expected of a young man but not for a man sixty-five-years-old, it shows his determination and a strong desire to provide for the stable establishment of his sons.  François had been involved in protests and the demands of an active lifestyle.  One would have expected him to be looking for a more tranquil life, not starting new careers.  To reward the former officers of the military, Jean Talon, the intendant of Canada granted a large land tract, which were established as seigneur lordships.  Jean Talon's successors followed the same practice and François Bélanger asked for and obtained a new grant for his land.
With this new grant, François and Marie gave their farm and buildings at L'Ange Gardien to their son Charles.  François new grant was located on the south side of the St. Lawrence River, where it seemed there was a better future.  This grant included title to the domain and a Seigneurie, which was granted to him by Louis de Buade, Count of Frontenac (the Governor) and his aide Duchesneau, on 1 Jul 1677.  This grant, called “Bonsecours”, consisted of a league (3 miles) of river frontage land by two leagues (6 miles) in depth on the south bank of the St. Lawrence River which, today, is part of the parish of L'Islet, Québec.

YEAR 1678 (8 paras)
============================================================
On 28 Jan 1678, forty-seven-year-old Nathaniel Gary died from smallpox in the town of Roxbury in the Massachusetts Bay Colony.  Nathaniel’s wife Anne Mattle (Douglas) was left raising their ten children, the oldest of which were seventeen-year-old twins daughters Mary and Elizabeth.  Thirteen-year-old daughter Sarah Gary was one of the ten children.  Widow Anne Mattle (Douglas) remarried five-years later in 1683 a Thomas Bishop in the town of Roxbury.  Anne and Thomas had no children.
On 11 Apr 1678, Eliezer Taylor was born to William Taylor and Hannah (Merriam).  Eliezer was named after the son of Deacon William Ward who was slain during “King Philip's War”.  Later on, 21 Jan 1680, William and Hannah had a second son William Jr., but the trauma of childbirth took the life of Hannah on the same day.  Yes, this was the second wife of William Taylor who died in child birth.
In Feb 1678, thirty-seven-year-old Agnès Bonhomme (Morin) was charged with “uttering insulting words against the Canada’s Governor Louis de Frontenac.”  Exactly what Agnès said or did not say is not clear in records, but she was arrested and made to stand trial.  Governor Frontenac himself may have pressed the charges, and Agnès said on the witness stand that her guards “ill-treated” her.  It was the Canada’s Sovereign Court that had control of the proceedings and after hearing several witnesses, the court dismissed the case.  Governor Frontenac wasn’t pleased and he took his rage out on the clerk who served the court.  First, he cornered the court clerk after a dinner and demanded he share what was going on in the closed court session.  The clerk refused and Governor Frontenac next forced the information out of the clerk and got even more angry when he learned the court proceeding were not going as he had hoped.  He ended up grabbing the clerk by his coat, and ordering his guards to lock him up; the clerk was detained for several days.  In the end Agnès was exonerated (freed) by the Sovereign Court.
On 27 Jun 1678,  Jean Pilote and Marie-Francoise Gaudry were married in Québec City in Canada.
On 19 Jul 1678, Jean Besset returned back to Chambly and purchased 3.4 acres of frontage abandoned by Arnaud Piat dit LaFleur in exchange for 70 livres.
On 5 Dec 1678, Françoise Agnès Gingras was born to Sébastien Gingras and Marie Genevieve (Guillebourg) in Sillery, Canada.
On 26 Dec 1678, Charles Carrier Sr. was born to Jean Carrier and Barbe (Halay) in Lauzon, Levis, Canada.
In 1678, Jean Dumay Jr. bought 13 acres of land for his brother Étienne Dumay Sr., repaying his brother for the house that Governor Pierre Dubois Davaugour gave Étienne in Québec City, which Étienne then gave to his brother Jean.

YEAR 1680 (7 paras)
============================================================
Note:  In the 1680’s a few wealthy and respectable businessmen in the Boston area began to purchase large tracts of unsettled land out west of the existing established colonial settlements.   The areas purchased were large, containing a number of square miles of land, with loosely defined boundaries.  Once the purchase was completed the new owner(s) would approach the Massachusett Bay Colony’s Colonial Governor and the General Court to confirm the title on the purchased land.  Most of the time the title was granted based on the condition that a certain number of families within a specified number of years would settle in the new land and a portion of the new settlement would be set aside for church and school lands and town buildings.
On 6 Jan 1666, twenty-seven-year-old Laurent Migneron in Chizé, Nouvelle-Aquitaine, France, where he was living and working as a “carpenter”, went before government official Claude Auber and gave everything he owned to Marie Monnier as a deposit to French King Louis XIV.  Shortly after the transaction was completed Laurent Migneron sailed over to Canada.  A mandate by King Louis XIV, exercised by Governor Francois Montmorency, granted Laurent Migneron a parcel of land 28 miles northeast of Quebec City in the parish of Saint Joachim of the community of Beaupré.  The land was registered later in 1680 as being located west of the Saint Anne River where it empties into the St. Lawrence River.
François Bélanger became the Lord of the Domain of “Bonsecours”.  Two of his sons, who had come with him to the south shore, left soon after to set up their own farms nearby.  After forty years of struggle and constant work, François had suddenly become the Seigneur of “Bonsecours) (L'Islet).  This concession was placed on record by the Sovereign Council on 24 Oct 1680.  Later in 1709, Gedeon de Catalogne (an engineer), after reading the description on the plans of seigneuries and lands of the governments of Québec, Trois Rivieres and Montreal, spoke of it as follows:  "The land there is rather level, sprinkled with plowed up stones, and marginally produces all sorts of grains, vegetables and pasturage.  The fruit trees produce abundantly, and the natural woods are a mixture of all species."
On 14 Jan 1680, Marie Agnes Pilote was born to Jean Pilote and Marie-Francoise (Gaudry) in L’Ancienne-Lorette, Canada.
In 1680, twenty-three-year-old Pierre Charles Le Sueur left the Jesuit mission in Sault Ste Marie, which was located on the St. Mary’s River that leaves Lake Superior and flows into Lake Huron to pursue the lure of the fur trade.  Pierre was soon involved in trade with the Sioux Nation on the upper Mississippi River, learning the skills need for living in the frontier.
In 1680, France the most powerful country in Europe, sent over to Canada the “Troupes de la Marine”, a regular military force under the command of the French Ministry of the Navy.  The troops were organized in France and sent to Canada.  Many of the soldiers eventually settled in Canada and replacements for the Troupe were recruited from young Canadians.  The units of the “Troupes de la Marine” were more professional than the local Canadian colonial militia.  The “Troupes de la Marine” quickly adapted the Native American-style forest warfare.  In 1687 the Marquis de Denonville launched an attack against Iroquois-Seneca villages with 832 soldiers, 1,030 militia and 800 Native American allies in the largest military operation to date.  The operation successfully pushed the Iroquois back to their home territory south of Lake Ontario  (Western New York).
On 6 Nov 1689, Ignace Gagne and Louise Tremblay were married in L’Ange-Gardien, Canada.  The marriage is the second for Ignace, who had married earlier Barbe Dodier on 5 Nov 1680 in Quebec City.  Barbe died in Feb 1689.  Ignace Gagne and his new wife Louise (Tremblay) move farther down the St Lawrence River to Baie-St-Paul, Canada.

On 10 Feb 1681, Daniel Rice Sr. and Bethiah Ward were married in the town of Marlborough in the Massachusetts Bay Colony.
About 1658, Toussaint Giroux and his wife Marie (Godard) had completed their 3-year servitude contract period working as “house servants” for Robert Giffard.  Toussaint bought a plot of land in the Bourg de Fargy section of Beauport where he built a house consisting of “a heated room, antechamber, cellar and attic.”  Toussaint was friends with Michel Baugis and the two men became “eel fishermen” together.  They were living next to each other in the 1666 census.  Toussaint Giroux was among 65 people confirmed at the church of Notre-Dame de Québec on 24 Feb 1660.  In 1681, Toussaint had 45 acres of land under cultivation.  Wife Marie (Godard) died on 21 Nov 1684 and Toussaint remarried Marie Therese (Leblanc) on 29 Oct 1686.  They had a daughter born two years later, but the marriage ended in a separation that was granted on 12 Apr 1691.  The reason for the ending of the marriage was because Marie Therese had spent a large sum of money shopping in Québec settlement and caused Toussaint to go into debt, so he arranged to end their marriage.
In 1667, Paul Vachon had two exceptional commissions entrusted to him in quick succession.  On 3 Nov 1667, Marie-Barbe de Boullongne, Governor General d’Ailleboust’s widow, appointed Paul Vachon a seigneurial attorney and notary in her Argentenay fief on the Île d’Orléans and a week later on 10 Nov 1667, Bishop Laval named Paul to the same offices in his Beauport and Île d’Orléans seigneuries.  Paul as an attorney, did not forget he came from country stock, and never failed to work his land.  In 1666 Paul had an “indentured domestic,” twenty-two-year-old Michel Aubin, who worked Paul’s farm where he raised 7 head of cattle and cultivated 20 acres of farmland.  Later in 1681 Paul still had an “indentured domestic”, 13 head of cattle, and 35 acres of farmland under cultivation.  In addition to his land at Beauport, Paul had another eight acres on the island of Île d’Orléans, which was worked by another tenant farmer.
Note: By 1681, Québec had grown into a city with over 1,300 people and a census was taken.

In 1681, Pierre Charles Le Sueur, who was a fur trapper and trader in the western Great Lakes area, was arrested and jailed in the Ville Marie settlement for trading fur pelts illegally.  Pierre was soon freed and in 1683 he had joined a party fur traders who left Ville Marie in 15 canoes and traveled across the Great Lakes to Green Bay, a large bay on the west side of Lake Michigan and then on to the headwater of the Mississippi River that Jean Nicolet had discover back in 1634.  Pierre found or received some samples of bluish clay from the upper Mississippi River, which he took France for analysis.  A French chemist, Alexandre L'Huillier, deemed the clay sample was high-grade copper ore.  Pierre Le Sueur returned to Canada to mine the copper, but was convicted of overreaching his trade privileges and was put in prison.  Eventually, however, Pierre was given a royal French commission to open a copper mine (although some suggested he was more interested in "mining animal furs").

YEAR 1682 (9 paras)
============================================================
The town of Roxbury in the Connecticut Colony had been an Indian Praying village called Wabaquasset that got involved in the “King Philip’s War”.  The Native people on the north side of the village had sided with King Philip and join in the fight against the English colonists.  The Native people living in the south side of the village favored the Mohegan tribe and joined on the English colonist’s side.  The Wabaquasset village was deserted during the war.  In 1682, the Massachusetts Bay Colony bought a large track of land from the Mohegan Native people, which include the old Wabaquasset Praying Village’s land.  In 1686, a group of 13 men from Roxbury, near Boston in the Massachusetts Bay Colony settled the new town and named it New Roxbury, 70 miles west of Boston in the Connecticut Colony.  Later in 1690, Judge Samuel Sewall suggested the town change its name to Woodstock in the Connecticut Colony.

On 27 Feb 1682, François Chrétien was born to Vincent Chrétien and Marie Anne (Leclerc) in the Saint Francois Parish on the Île d’Orléans Island in Canada.

On 24 Oct 1682, Joseph Bonneau and Maurice Arrive’ signed a contract with Louis Rouer to build a house with stone walls in the Lower-Town of Québec.  The partners were paid 5 livres for each “toise” (2.13 sq yards) of masonry put down.

YEAR 1683 (5 paras)
============================================================
In 1659, twenty-two-year-old William Sterling and Elizabeth Sawtelle were married in the town of Haverhill in the Massachusetts Bay Colony.  The record of the marriage is the first record identifying William Sterling was now living in the English American Colonies.  Elizabeth Sawtelle was the first of William’s four wives.  William Sterling appears in the 1662 records of Essex County in the Massachusetts Bay Colony, where the birth of his first child was entered as born in the town of Rowley in the Massachusetts Bay Colony.  The town of Rowley was 13-miles east of Haverhill.  Between 1660 and 1664, William Sterling was taxed each year three shillings and ten pence by the town of Rowley.  William worked as a ship ”carpenter and grain miller”.  He was living in the Rowley in 1669, when he sold his property to Stephen Kent in exchange for the latter's house, barn and orchard in the town of Haverhill, on the Merrimac River, near a small stream called Little River.  William Sterling secured another twelve acres of land sometime prior to 1683 on the site where the city hall of the town of Haverhill, Massachusetts now stands, and built on the land a two-story house.  In the town of Haverhill, William was a “carpenter” by trade and built ships on the Merrimac River for the New England coastal traders.  He was a member of the “Board of Selectmen” in Haverhill.  William offered his house and land to the town as a home for the town’s minister.  He received in payment, ten acres of land on Fishing River, and the use of ten more acres for a grain mill he erected.  William was prominent in Haverhill town’s affairs, and from 1693 to 1696 he was one of the town’s “tithingman” (English Peace Officer) and also held the office of the town’s “Constable”, responsible for serving court documents, assisting at executions of writs (collection court fines) and maintaining order during court sessions
On 28 Nov 1683, widower William Taylor married his third wife widow Mary Cheevers in the town of Cambridge in the Massachusetts Bay Colony.  The marriage was the second for Mary who had earlier married William Lewis when she was fifteen-year-old and he was fifty-one-years-old.  Mary did not have had any children with he first husband.  William Taylor and Mary (Cheevers) had nine children, including daughter Elizabeth Taylor.  Mary (Cheevers) died later on 28 Jul 1697 giving birth to her ninth child daughter Marcy Taylor, who also died five months later.  Yes, this was William’s third wife to die giving birth to a child.  William Taylor remarried for the fourth time 1699, widow Sarah Parker (Wheeler) and they had two more children.
On 19 May 1683, Antoine Dubois sold his license to trap beavers to Augustin Lafond for £150, payable in beaver pelts.
On 24 Oct 1683, Joseph Bonneau and Maurice Arrive’ who had earned the reputation of being good masons were hired by Jean Lerouge another mason and a surveyor.
In 1683, Jean Pilote after signing a contract with Aubert de la Chesnaye and accompanied by four other Frenchmen left on a fur trade expedition to the Illinois wilderness.  Upon their return, with a large load fur pelts, the group of Frenchmen were ambushed and taken prisoners by Iroquois warriors, who confiscated their seven canoes, their fur pelts and all their provisions.  The Iroquois next escorted the group to Fort St-Louis outside of Québec City where they were released.  The Iroquois’ left the five Frenchmen fur-trappers with only one rifle and a little powder.

YEAR 1684 (16 paras)
============================================================
On 3 Jun 1684, Daniel Rice Jr. was born to Daniel Rice Sr. and Bethiah (Ward) in the town of Marlborough in the Massachusetts Bay Colony.
In 1684, John Leavens Jr. and his wife Elizabeth (Preston) left the town of Stratford in the Connecticut Colony and move to the town of Roxbury in the Connecticut Colony where they were received into the Roxbury Church 22 Apr 1684.  John had become a “master carpenter”.
On 23 Oct 1684, the “Massachusetts Bay Company Charter” was revoked by King Charles II after repeated violations of the charter’s terms, including discrimination against Anglicans and Quakers and running an illegal mint (making money).
In the Summer 1673, Noël Langlois Jr., called himself, “Sieur de Traversy”.  Noël Jr. loved to hunt, fish and the military life and he accompanied Governor Frontenac on an expedition west to the area of present-day Kingston, at the beginning of the St. Lawrence River where it leaves Lake Ontario.  Because Noël Jr. distinguished himself on this venture Governor Frontenac promised him a Seigneury.  When reminded of his promise, Frontenac granted a fief on the south shore of the St. Lawrence River.  Noël Langlois Jr. called his land “Port Joli”.  It was not good farmland, but was excellent for the hunting and fishing, which Noël Jr. enjoyed.  It also contained old large oak trees that provided large oak spars, used to hold up a ship’s sails, for his brothers' shipbuilding business.  Noël Jr. never was able to develop his Seigneury.  His father Noël Langlois Sr. was old and wanted him to stay at home to look after him and his second wife.  To this end Noël Langlois Sr. assigned all his assets and debts to the son.  To straighten out the tangled affairs left by his father, Noël Jr. sold his Seigneury “Port Joli”.  Noël Jr. could have kept the Seigneury if he had sold the homestead in Beauport after the death of the father in 1684, but he chose not to.
On 13 Feb 1684, thirty-one-year-old Marie-Anne Lelong, first wife of Joseph Bonneau died from a bad case of influenza.  Joseph Bonneau and Marie-Anne had five children: Pierre, Joseph Jr., Marie-Jeanne, Anne and Antoine.  Early deaths affected all five of Joseph and Marie-Anne’s children.  Son Pierre died at age 26, son Joseph Jr. and his two children died from a smallpox epidemic in 1702, daughter Marie-Jeanne lost her husband after one year of marriage and spent the rest of her life in service to the Seminary of Québec, and youngest four-year-old daughter Anne died and the last son Antoine died at age twenty-two from a great pestilence (a disease endemic).
On 27 Feb 1684, Seigneur Crevier de Saint-François granted Antoine Dubois 5 acres of frontage property in the Saint-François Parish on the l'Île-d'Orléans island. The land ran across the entire width of the island.
On 11 Apr 1684, thirty-five-year-old widower Joseph Bonneau and seventeen-year-old Madeleine Duchesne were married in the Saint-François Parish on l'Île-d'Orléans Island in Canada.  At this time Joseph Bonneau had taken on the bit name “LaBecasse” which meant a “woodcock bird” in French.  The Bonneau family was well known for hunting of snipe ground birds.  The Snipe are notoriously hard to hunt, hence the expression “Off on a snipe hunt” means “off wasting your time”.
On 2 Oct 1684, Joseph Bonneau contracted to make a fireplace in the home of Abraham Methot, in return for 1,600 pickled eels, which Joseph shared with his partner Maurice Arrive.
On 30 Apr 1684, Mathurin Lebeau was born to Jean Lebeau and Etiennette (Loriot) in Fort St-Louis, Chambly in Canada.  Fort St-Louis was built back in 1655 by Captain Jacques de Chambly an officer in the “Carignan-Salières Regiment” to protect the Canada colonists from Native Iroquois warrior attacks.
In 1684, Jerome Longtin Sr. left Paris, France and sailed to Canada and then traveled up the St. Lawrence River to the Ville Marie settlement in Canada.

In 1685, the “Dominion of New England” was created by English Parliament through administrative action that united all the New England Colonies and Mid-Atlantic Colonies, except for the Pennsylvania Colony.  The colonies deeply resented the union since their rights and colony charters were revoked by the process.  The specific objectives of the new dominion included: regulation of trade, reformation of land title practices to conform more to the English methods and practices, coordination on matters of defense, and a streamlining of the government administration into fewer centers.

The new town was named Rutland, Massachusetts in the Dominion of New England.
In 1686, England’s King James II appointed the former New York, Colony governor Sir Edmund Andros as the new “Dominion of New England” governor. In 1686, Sir Edmund Andros, as the governor of the new Dominion of New England, proposed new taxes, pushing aside the Massachusett General Court and forbidding all town meetings.

In addition, many of the Native Americans who were forced flee north to Maine area after “King Philip’s War” now sought revenge and joined with Wabanaki Confederacy tribes, which were allied with the French against the English colonists in Maine.

YEAR 1689 (5 paras)
============================================================
On 18 April 1689, the colonists in the City of Boston rose up against the “Dominion of New England” rule.  A well-organized "mob" descended on the City of Boston and arrested the English Dominion government officials.  Governor Sir Edmund Andros had his quarters in Fort Mary, a garrison house on the south side of Boston where he and a number of other former Dominion officials took refuge.  The old Massachusetts Bay Colony leadership headed by ex-governor Simon Bradstreet was quickly restored.  English Dominion Governor Sir Edmund Andros was ordered by the restored Massachusetts Bay Colony leadership to surrender, for his own safety.  He refused, and tried to escape to an English ship the “Rose” anchored in the Boston harbor.  A small rowboat sent from the “Rose” was intercepted by colonial militia, and governor Andros was forced back into Fort Mary.  Negotiations ensued, and Governor Andros agreed to leave the fort to meet with the Massachusett rebel council.  Promised safe conduct, Governor Andros was marched under guard to the townhouse where the rebel council had assembled.  There he was told that "they must & would have the Government in their own hands", and that he was under arrest.  Sir Edmund Andros was taken to the home of former dominion treasurer John Usher, and held under close watch.  When Fort Mary fell into rebel hand on 19 April 1689 Governor Andros was move out to Castle Island in the harbor.  On 2 Aug 1689, Andros escaped and fled to Rhode Island, where he was recaptured and sent to England for trial.  Massachusetts Bay Colony agents in London refused to sign the charges made against Sir Edmund Andros, so the English court summarily dismissed the charges.
On 4 Aug 1689 more than 1,500 Mohawk Iroquois warriors attacked the island of Île de Montréal at the village of Lachine, in revenge for the attack on the Seneca two years earlier. The attack was encouraged by the English, since England was now at war with France.  The Lachine village had been established about nine miles upstream (west) from Ville-Marie settlement, above the Lachine Rapids, back in 1667.  The Lachine village burned to the ground and an estimated 80 French settlers were killed.  Jean Baptiste Neveu and Catherine (Gaudin) somehow survived the attack or had left Lachine before the attack.
In 1689, Pierre Charles Le Sueur was assisting Nicolas Perrot at Fort Antoine, (lower Lake Pepin on the Mississippi) when Perrot ceremonially took possession of the Mississippi's headwaters for France.
In 1689, the “King William’s War”, started in North America between France and England.  The war was a continuation of the war that was going on in Europe at the time between France and England.  The English captured Port Royal in Acadia from France with little resistance.  A year later in Oct 1690, the “Battle of Québec” was fought between Canada and British Massachusetts Bay Colony’s militia.  It was the first time Québec's defenses were tested.  Following the easy capture of Port Royal, the British New Englanders hoped to seize Québec itself, the capital of Canada.  The loss of the Acadian fort shock the French and Governor-General Louis de Buade de Frontenac ordered the immediate preparation in the Québec City for siege.  The English New England forces surrounded the fortified City of Québec.  When a New Englander envoy delivered the terms of surrender to the city, the Governor-General famously declared that his only reply would be by "the mouth of my cannons”.  On 18 Oct 1690, English Sir William Phips started bombarding the Québec City with cannons on 37 English war ships in the St Lawrence River.  The shelling had little effect, and after a defeat at Beauport three days later, the English retreat.  The expedition's ships, commanded by Sir William Phips, were nearly destroyed by French cannons on the Quebec City walls.  See below a painting of the “Battle of Québec”:
In 1689, during an Iroquois raid on a large Miami Native settlement, in what will become the State of Illinois, the Iroquois’ destroyed the settlement and captured a large number of Miami native prisoners.  The Miami tribe asked for help from the other members of the Anishinaabe Confederation, which were native tribes like the Miami, which spoke the Anishinaabe language that was part of the larger Algonquian language group.  A large group of other native tribes joined with the Miami and Illinois tribes to confront the ongoing Iroquois raiding menace that impact the lives of all the peaceful native tribes in the region.  A large combined force of the Anishinaabe Confederation, using newly acquired European firearms tracked down the Iroquois raiders camped near the southernmost bend of the St. Joseph River (called South Bend Indiana today).  The combined Anishinaabe Confederation forces overwhelmed the Iroquois killing almost all of the Iroquois warriors.  The result of the destruction of the Iroquois raiding force was the creation of a large depopulated region that the Iroquois had controlled.  The Iroquois were never able to reestablish a permanent presence in the area and overtime the original native tribes used the opportunity to slowly return to their ancestral homes. YEAR 1691 (7 paras)
============================================================
On 7 Oct 1691, England’s new King William and Queen Mary II rechartered the Massachusetts Bay Colony into the new “Province of Massachusetts Bay”.  The new province charter allowed England to take control over the former Massachusetts Bay Colony.  A new English governor Sir William Phips was placed in charge of the new province.  The province included the Massachusetts Bay Colony, the Plymouth Colony, the Province of Maine, the islands of Martha’s Vineyards and Nantucket, Nova Scotia and New Brunswick.  Later in 1697, Nova Scotia and New Brunswick were separated from the Province of Massachusetts.  The new charter restricted religious-based laws, such as the church membership requirement to become a voter, extended religious tolerance to other Protestant denominations other than the Anglican Church of England , required oaths to be taken to the king and not the government of Massachusetts and tightened the English government’s overall control over the colony.  The Puritans in the colonies worried that their religion and themselves were once again under attack.
In 1691, the new “Province of Massachusetts Bay” that was formed did not include New Hampshire.  The separate New Hampshire became a new English Crown Colony.  Often, however the “Province of Massachusetts Bay” and the New Hampshire Colony shared the same English appointed governor.
On 28 Oct 1691, Samuel Green and Elizabeth Upham were married in the town of Malden in the Province of Massachusetts Bay.
In 1691, the Province of Massachusetts became famous for its “witch trials”.  The witch hysteria started in the town of Chelmsford, which was 30 miles west of Salem, when Martha (Barrett) Sparks was accused of witchcraft and on 28 Oct 1691 was sent to a Boston prison.  Martha remained in prison for over a year until 6 Dec 1692 at the start of the first legal actions leading to the “Salem Witch Trials”.  Martha Sparks who was the granddaughter of Thomas Barrett Sr. and Margaret (Huntington) was never brought to trial.  Thirty-five-year-old Martha was the daughter of Thomas Barrett Jr. and Frances (Woolderson) of the town of Braintree, Massachusetts.  Martha was married to Henry Sparks, who was away fighting in the “King Philip’s War”, when Martha was accused and put in prison on suspicion of witchcraft.  The History of Chelmsford provides an account of John Arnold, the Boston jailer, who not only played host to Martha Sparks for 58 weeks, but also had as his guests the more celebrated ladies from Salem, including Sarah Good, Sarah Osbourn and Rebecca Nurse.
On 20 May 1774, the British Parliament passed the “Massachusetts Government Act” that revoked the 1691 charter of the Province of Massachusetts Bay and appointed a new British Royal governor over the former province with wide-ranging powers.  The Massachusetts colonial leaders argued the act altered the basic historical structure of American colonial government and vehemently opposed the new act and would not let it operate.
On 11 Aug 1691, the “Battle of La Prairie” occurred when 120 English militiamen and 146 Native warriors from the Mohawk and Mohican tribes attacked the French colonial settlement of La Prairie in Canada.   The French governor, responded by amassing 700 French militiamen and Native American allies at Fort La Prairie, located on the south shore of the St. Lawrence River.  The fort had been built three years earlier to protect the new and growing La Prairie French colonial settlement.
On 18 Oct 1691, Dominique Bonneau Sr. was born to Joseph Bonneau and Madeleine (Duchesne) in Montmorency, Canada.
