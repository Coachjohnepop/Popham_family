YEAR 1626 (18 paras)
============================================================

In 1626, the Gloucester settlement at Cape Ann failed and the settlers headed by Roger Conant moved to the nearly abandoned Naumkeag Native American settlement in the area, which later was renamed Salem, without first obtaining permission from the King Charles I.  Although the Cape Ann settlement was established as a business venture, one of the founders of the Cape Ann settlement, Reverend John White, also wanted the settlement to be a place of refuge for Puritans escaping religious persecution back in England.

On 1 Jan 1626, John Douglas Alcock was born to George Alcock and Anne (Hooker) in Markfield, Leicestershire, England.

On 1 Nov 1626, Deacon John Upham and Elizabeth Webb were married in Bicton, Devonshire, England.

In 1626, Robert Smith was born to Robert Smith Sr. and Margaret Deane in Boston, Lincolnshire, England.

In 1626, Solomon Johnson and Eleanor Crofts were married in London, Middlesex, England.

In 1628, the “London Plantation” obtained a patent to settle the Salem area in Cape Cod and took over the illegal settlement established there by the colonists from the failed Gloucester settlement back in 1626.

On 15 Apr 1626, Françoise Morin was born to Jean Morin and Jeanne (Desnquets) in Gahard, Brittany, France.

On 19 Jun 1626, Anne Cloutier was born to Zacharie Cloutier and Saintes (Dupont) in Mortagne, Perche, Normandy, France.

In 1626, Pierre Tremblay was born to Philibert Tremblay and Jeanne (Coignet) in Randonnai, Tourouvre au Perche, France.

In 1626, Pierre Saint-Denis was born to Paschal Saint-Denis and Rosalie (Daoust) in Dieppe, Normandy, France.

In 1626, Vivienne Brunel was born to Louis Brunelle and Nathalie (Campeau) in Rouen, Normandy, France.

In 1626, Etienne Dumay Sr. was born to Jean Dumay and Miotte (Lecombe) in Dieppe, Normandy, France.

In 1626, Sébastienne Veillon was born to Maxient Veillon and Bernarde (Venet) in Verdille in Poitou, which is 10 miles from La Rochelle, Normandy, France.

On 6 Aug 1626, Marguerite Couillard was born to Guillaume Couillard and Guilleumette (Hébert) in the French Québec settlement.

On 1626, Louis Hébert was granted additional land and along the Saint Charles River in recognition of his “painful labors” by the new Viceroy Samuel de Champlain.

On 1626, Samuel de Champlain established the first farm area in Château-Richer community on the north side of the St. Lawrence River across from the Île d'Orléans Island.

In 1626, the Native American Iroquois Confederation destroyed the Mohicans tribe and became the dominate native force in eastern North America south of the St. Lawrence River.  They next set their sights on the land north of the St. Lawrence River.

The French became interested in the Senegal area, particularly the Senegal River and the large river valley.  In 1626 France set up of a monopoly company the “Norman Company” made up of merchants from Dieppe and Rouen areas of France to exclusive trade with the people of Senegal.  The “Norman Company” was dissolved later in 1658 and replace by new “Company of Cape Vert and Senegal”.  The start and stop of a series of monopole trading companies occurred in Senegal the same way it occurred in Canada during the same period.  Just as happened in Canada, Frenchmen who went to work in the trading monopolies in Senegal were not allowed to take French wives and Frenchwomen were also not allow to travel to the French Colony.  The young adventured single Frenchmen partnered or married native women in Senegal as occurred in Canada.  See a map on the next page showing the areas where the Moors/Berber people lived.

YEAR 1627 (13 paras)
============================================================

In 1627, the bickering directors of the London Merchants Adventures Company sold out their interests in the “Plimoth Plantation” to the settlers living in the plantation for £1,800.  The settlers spent the next fifteen years paying off the debt.

On 5 Feb 1627, Richard Sawtelle and Elizabeth Pople were married in High Ham, Somerset, England.

On 10 Oct 1627, Ralph Farnham Sr. and Alice Harris were married in Southampton, England.

In Mar 1627, John Endecott was one of seven signatories to a land grant given to "The New England Company for a Plantation in Massachusetts" (or the New England Company) by the Earl of Warwick on behalf of the Plymouth Council for New England.  The Plymouth Council was at the time the umbrella organization overseeing English colonization efforts in North America between 40- and 48-degrees latitude on the coast of North America.

Note:  From 1613 to 1620, the “Compagnie des Marchands“ was a monopoly company supported by the French government that operated in Canada but failed to fulfill their contractual obligations and therefore lost their monopoly rights in 1621 to the “ Compagnie de Montmorency”.  Six years later in 1627, French Cardinal Richelieu, the real leader of France withdrew the monopoly of the “Compagnie de Montmorency”, and established in its place the Compagnie de la Nouvelle France” as part of a plan to develop and expand trade in Canada.  Throughout all of this period the monopoly holders were frequently troubled with rogue traders (from France and other nations) in North America on one side, and politically connected opponents of their monopoly in France on the other side.  Many of the directors of these companies were more interested in trade than in colonization, which was usually a drain on the company's finances.  Samuel de Champlain, who championed the colonization efforts, worked tirelessly to make sure the French colonies survived amid political and corporate changes of power.

On 25 Jan 1627, fifty-two-year-old Louis Hébert, the first farmer of Quebec, died from a fall on ice and his widow wife Marie was left to manage the farm with help from son-in-law Guillaume Couillard, the husband of her daughter Guilleumette (Hébert).

On 26 Dec 1627, Jean Guay was born to Jean Guay Sr. and Marie (Dumont) in Berneuil, Nouvelle-Aquitaine, France.

In 1627, following the death of Louis’s Hébert, his second son Francois Hébert and Guillaume Couillard each inherited half of Louis Hébert’s and estate.  Samuel de Champlain granted Guillaume Couillard an additional one hundred acres of land bordering on the Saint Charles River.  The next spring Guillaume became the first person to use a plow in the colony as he prepared his new land for farming.

In 1627, France’s Catholic Cardinal Richelieu, the chief minister and powerful authority with great influence over France’s King Louis XIII, got interested and became involved in the Canada Colony.  During this period Robert Giffard left the Québec settlement and returned to France to encourage others from the Perche area in Normandy France to immigrate to Canada and start a new life.  Robert Giffard received approval from Cardinal Richelieu, to form the “Company of the One Hundred-Associates” with a charter that gave the company a monopoly on the French business enterprise actives in Canada, to manage the booming fur trade and control land rights in the Canada colony.  Canada quickly became a new source of beaver fur, which was used to make the popular wide-brimmed felt hats for the French aristocracy and the Parisian bourgeoisie (those in the county with wealth and ownership of the production of goods).  The fur hats even became part of the standard French military uniform.  The process of felt making involved converted the beaver fir into a material that was warm, soft, pliable and weather-resistant.  The “Company of the One Hundred-Associates” as a government monopoly, was required to send 4,000 settlers to Canada over the next 15 years.  The Company largely ignored the settlement requirements and focused on the lucrative fur trade instead.

In 1627, Catholic Cardinal Richelieu implemented through the “Company of the One Hundred-Associates” a new restriction allowing only Catholics to immigrate to the Canada Colony.  This new rule resulted in all French Protestant Huguenots leaving Canada and going to the Dutch and English colonies in North America, limiting a source of energetic hardworking settlers who were just the people needed to grow the French Canada Colony.

In 1627, the Québec settlement had grown to 100 people, of which only 12 were women.  A new wooden fort had been constructed.  After the last supply ship from France arrived, no additional supply ships or new emigrants reach Canada over the next two years.  War with England had broken out and France forgot about its Canada Colony.

In 1627, the “Anglo-French War” broke out, between France and England when the English attacked the fort at La Rochelle in France in support of the French Protestant Huguenots.  The Huguenots were fighting against King Louis XIII of France, who was trying to bring the Huguenots under his Catholic control.  The city of La Rochelle had become the stronghold of the French Protestant Huguenots.  It was the center of Huguenot Sea power and the strongest center of resistance against the King Louis XIII and his central Catholic government.  The English used the war to launched a campaign against France's new Canada colony in North America, which led to the English capture of the Québec settlement.  The clashes between the Catholics and Protestants in France resulted in fierce violence, despite the “Edict of Nantes” signed back in 1598 by French King Henry IV that granted considerable religious and civil liberty to French Protestant Huguenots.  The “Siege of La Rochelle” lasted fifteen months.  The town resisted in a heroic fashion but had to surrender to the French Army in 1628 after 15,000 of its 20,000 inhabitants had died.

In 1627, the “Seigneurial System” (Manor System) was instituted in Canada that granted land to seigneurs: usually military leaders or aristocrats just below the French Ruling Class, including high ranking church leaders.  The seigneurs were charged with the task of subdividing large parcels of land and renting out sections to peasant families who would engage in subsistence farming to meet most of their food, heating, and shelter needs.  Under regulations set up by the French government, the seigneur could also set up a court of law and a mill on his land and organize a collective farm.  The seigneurial system was based on the European feudal system, were plots of land were given to noblemen - who were called seigneurs - in return for their loyalty to the king and a promise to perform military service when necessary.  The system created a traditional peasant-lord relationship and established an upper group of landed elite.  This system of land distribution was regulated by French law.  Its purpose was to promote settlement in a systematic way.  Seigneuries areas, which were usually (3 miles by 9 miles) in size, were generally divided into river lots.  The long, rectangular strips facilitated interaction between neighbors and provided multiple points of access to the river, the principal communication route of goods and people.  The seigneur’s system doomed the French in North America to their eventual defeat against the British who allowed their settlers to own their own land and get ahead through hard work.  The ability to acquire and own land in the English American Colonies attracted millions of settlers hoping for a better life.  Canada on the other hand struggled to attract new settlers, which in the end caused them to be overwhelmed by the large number English.  See the map below of the Île-d’Orléans Island showing how the typical Seigneur’s land was subdivided into land parcels with waterfront access.

YEAR 1628 (12 paras)
============================================================

In 1624, William Perkins is admitted to the Emmanuel College in Cambridge, England.  A year later William enrolled in Cambridge where he received his B.A degree in 1628 from Christ’s College.

On 20 Jun 1628 the ship “Abigail” sailed from Weymouth, Dorset, England and arrived on 6 Sep 1628 in Salem, with a new government for the “London Plantation”, under Governor Captain John Endicott.

In 1628 Thomas Green Sr. and Elizabeth Lynde were married in England.

The beginning of the Connecticut Colony began back in 1610 when the Dutch established the first trading post on the Connecticut River Valley in what would become the town of Hartford.  The word "Connecticut" is a French corruption of the Native American word “quinetucket”, which means "beside the long, tidal river”.  The later move into the Connecticut River Valley was part of a general movement out of the Massachusetts Bay Colony.  By the mid-1630s, the population in and around Boston had grown so dense that settlers began to move out throughout southern New England, concentrating on navigable river valleys such as Connecticut River.  The man credited as the founder of the English Connecticut Colony was Rev. Thomas Hooker, an English yeoman, and clergyman born in 1586 in Markfield, Leicester, England.  He was educated at Cambridge College in England and was one of the most well-educated and powerful preachers of both old and New England.  He was a nonconformist Puritan who was targeted for suppression by the English government under King Charles I and was forced to flee to Holland.  Governor John Winthrop of the Massachusetts Bay Colony wrote to Rev. Hooker in 1628, asking him to come to Massachusetts.  Rev. Thomas Hooker arrived in 1633 and was made pastor of the town church in Newton in the Massachusetts Bay Colony.

On 30 Jan 1660, after the fall of the Cromwell’s dictatorships in England, the son of the dead King Charles I was placed on the throne and became King Charles II.  The new King was forced to agree to a restoration agreement with the English Parliament, which gave the king a standing army and allowed him to purge the officials responsible for his father’s execution.  In exchange King Charles II was force agreed to honor the “Petition of Right” and accept limited income.  The “Petition of Right” is a major document in England’s constitutional set of laws that sets out specific liberties of the English subjects (people) that the king is prohibited from infringing upon.  Passed on 7 Jun 1628, the Petition contains restrictions on non-Parliamentary taxation, forced billeting of soldiers, imprisonment without cause, and the use of martial law.

In April 1628, the “Company of the One Hundred-Associates” sent a fleet of ships from Dieppe France with supplies and settlers heading to Canada.  The French fleet was intercepted by English ships commanded by David Kirke, Thomas Kirke and Louis Kirke “the Kirke’s brothers”.  A number of the French ships were sunk, and the rest were forced to return to France.  No French ships made it to Canada in 1628.

In 1628, Robert Giffard with his new wife Marie Renouard sailed back to Canada to prepare for the immigrants they would soon be bringing from the Perche area of France.  Their ship was also intercepted by English pirates and forced to return to France.

In 1628, Euphrosine Madeleine Nicolet was born as a Métis child (mixed Native and European blood child) to Jean Nicolet and Jeanne Bahmahmaadjimiwin Giisis in a Nipissing tribe settlement.  Folklore claims Jeanne B. Giisis was a Native Princess, the daughter of the Nipissing Chief.  The Nipissing Native people lived around Lake Nipissing, which is now in Ontario Québec, north of today’s city of Toronto and east of Lake Huron.

In 1628, Jeanne Crissot was born to unknown parents in Paris, Île-de-France, France.

About 1628, Jean Bonneau and Catherine Poulet were married in Mesnil, Hauts-de-France, France.

In 1628, Jean Lemarché was born to Jean Lemarché and Marie (Blondeau) in Paris, Île-de-France, France.

In the summer of 1628, the English ships appeared in the harbor of the Tadoussac outpost, 130 miles down the St. Lawrence River from the Québec settlement.  In July the English ships sailed up the St. Lawrence River and attacked the French farm at Cap Tourmente, 30 miles east of the Québec settlement.  The Frenchmen at the farm were all killed  and their farm structures were plundered, the livestock slaughtered and their homes were burn down.  On 10 Jul 1628, the English leaders, the three Kirke brothers, sent a letter to Samuel de Champlain demanding the surrender of the Québec settlement.  Samuel was able to bluff the English into thinking the small Québec settlement was stronger than it was and stopped English from attacking for the rest of the year.  The English were however successful in stopping all French support and supply ships from aiding the beleaguered and desperate Québec settlement.

YEAR 1629 (9 paras)
============================================================

In Mar 1629, King Charles I dismissed the English Parliament.  It would not be restored for eleven years, which greatly limited the king’s ability to raise funds for his government.

On 4 Mar 1629, the “London Plantation” renamed itself the “Massachusetts Bay Company” after the tribe of Massachusetts Native people living in New England.  The new company was granted a charter by King Charles I to officially engage in trade in New England.  King Charles I was happy to rid England of the Protestant trouble makers that were an obstacle his desire to return England to the traditional Catholic Church religious practices.  The King Charles I hoped most of the militant Protestant Puritans would leave England for the colonies in America and many did.

In Aug 1629, when the charter was issued to the new Massachusetts Bay Company for the “Massachusetts Bay Colony”, the charter neglected to say the company members had to stay in England to conduct their meetings.  The company held a series of meeting in Cambridge England where they voted to take advantage of the omission in the charter and move the entire company over to New England.

In Oct 1629, John Winthrop the spiritual and political leader of the Massachusetts Bay Company was elected by the company’s General Court as the first governor of the “Massachusetts Bay Colony”, while he was still in England.  The General Count was made up of the company’s investor shareholders (called “Freemen”) and operated as a governing body for the company.  John Winthrop would serve twelve annual terms as governor and was a force of comparative moderation in the religiously conservative colony.  John Winthrop was a respected political figure, his attitude toward governance was somewhat authoritarian: he resisted attempts to widen voting and other civil rights beyond a narrow class of religiously approved individuals, (Freemen) and opposed attempts to codify a body of laws that the colonial magistrates would be bound by, and also opposed unconstrained democracy, calling it "the meanest and worst of all forms of government".

In 1646, Ralph Shepard and his family left the town of Rehoboth and relocate to the town of Malden, Massachusetts where Ralph served as the Deacon of the Church in Malden.  The Malden area was a hilly woodland area north of Mystic River that had been purchased from the Pennacook Native tribe back in 1629.  The area had been called “Mistick Slide”, the name was changed by Joseph Hills to Malden after the town of Malden, Essex, England.

On 21 Aug 1629, Jacques Ménard was born to Jean Ménard and Anne (Savinelle) in Fontenay, Normandy, France.

On 9 Aug 1629, Samuel de Champlain formally surrendered his beloved Québec settlement to the English Kirke brothers.  When the English came ashore to raise the English flag, they found the colony had only one tub of potatoes and roots left to feed the 67 people living in Colony.   The settlement’s residents were close to starving.  On 14 Sep 1629, Samuel and most settlers, including  Abraham Martin, wife Marguerite (Langlois) and their fifteen-year-old daughter Anne Martin boarded ships and left Québec and sailed back to England.  The French settlement captives in the ships stopped at the Tadoussac outpost before setting sail across the Atlantic Ocean where Samuel learned that two Frenchmen had helped the Kirke’s navigate up the St. Lawrence River in their quest to seize Canada from the French.  Samuel was outraged by the discovery that Jacques Michel was a traitor to the country but, even more so, by the fact that Samuel’s own former servant, Étienne Brulé, was the other traitor!  Champlain felt deeply betrayed by his fellow countrymen.

In 1629, when Québec was captured by the English, Jean Nicolet, who was loyal to France, took refuge with Nipissing Native people in the Huron country.  He thwarted all the English plans to get the Native tribes to trade with them.  Jean Nicolet’s partner (lived together with but was not married) Jeanne Bahmahmaadjimiwin Giisis, a native Nipissing woman, with whom he had a daughter Euphrosine Madeleine Nicolet the year before.  Euphrosine  grew up to be an important native interpreter and intermediary between the New French and the local native tribes of the St. Lawrence River Valley.

In the fall of 1629, after returning to France, nine-year-old Hélène Desportes was living in the port city of Dieppe, France, the birth place of Hélène’s mother Françoise Langlois, who had passed away a few months earlier, and her aunt, Marguerite Martin (Langlois).  Marguerite and her current husband Abraham Martin were also living in Dieppe.

YEAR 1630 (25 paras)
============================================================

The format of the story of Winifred Eloise Coss’ Family Tree merges important historical events and notes within the family history of Winifred Coss’ relatives.  Winifred’s family tree is separated into two branches.  The people covered in the first branch of Winifred Coss’ Family are from England starting with George Alcock who was born in 1581 in Cambridge, Cambridgeshire, England.  The first branch ends with the marriage of Warren Coss and Edith Powers.  The early members of the Power’s family arrive in the ships of the Winthrop Fleet in 1630 in the new Massachusetts Bay Colony.  A second branch of Winifred Eloise Coss’ Family Tree begin in 1575 when Louis Hébert was born in Paris, Île-de-France and ends when Joseph Warren Coss marries Mary Goodwater.  The early members of the Goodwater family help establish the Québec settlement in Canada, New France.  Many other direct family members will be covered as other marriages occur.

In April 1630, the Puritans, led by John Winthrop, left their homes in Boston, Lincolnshire, England and set sail from Southampton, England to the New World.  King Charles I, had given the Massachusetts Bay Company the rights to the land between the Merrimack River on north and the Charles River on the south.  The territory ostensibly administered by the Massachusetts Bay Colony covered much of central New England, including portions of Massachusetts, Maine, New Hampshire, and Connecticut.  See a map below of the new Massachusetts Bay Colony.  The original Native tribes in the area are also identified on the map.

In Jun 1630, the first of eleven ships of the Winthrop Fleet arrived in Salem in the Massachusetts Bay Colony.  About 2,000 Puritans plus livestock and provisions arrived from England over the summer during this first phase of the “Great Migration”.  The settlement in Salem, which was occupied by members of the failed Cape Ann settlement quickly realized it did not have enough food or shelter to accommodate the newly arriving Puritans.  The Puritans were forced to move on and initially settled in Charlestown, across the river from the Shawmut peninsula.  Although they had a location, the colony still suffered due to a lack of fresh water.  By chance a college friend of John Winthrop, William Blackstone, was living on the Shawmut peninsula.  When William Blackstone heard of Winthrop’s arrival, he invited the Puritans to live with him on the Shawmut peninsula.  John Winthrop accepted the offer and the Puritans relocated and began construction of their new settlement, in what later would become the town of Boston.  Six new towns were laid out by the Massachusetts Bay Colony for the new settlers: Boston, Watertown, Lynn (Saugus), Newton (Cambridge), Dorchester, and Toxbury.

Between 1630 and 1640, an estimated twenty-five thousand Puritans left England and settled in the Massachusetts Bay Colony because they believed the corrupted morals and doctrines of the Church of England needed "purifying”.  Imprisoned and brutally persecuted for conscience’s sake, and seeing no hope for religious freedom in England, the braver of the Puritans sailed over to the New World.  The difference between the ‘Pilgrims’ and ‘Puritans’, who were also Protestants, differed in their opposition to the Anglian Church of England.  Both groups followed the teaching of John Calvin, but the Pilgrims were Puritans who had abandoned their local parishes and formed small congregations of their own because the Church of England was not holy enough to meet their standards.  The Pilgrims were labeled “Separatists”.

The newly arriving Puritans, who came in the 1630’s migration, were shocked at the primitive conditions facing them.  The ships they sailed on were freight-carrying vessels that were not designed for human passengers.  Two hundred of the Puritans who arrived in New England immediately went back to England.  The more steadfast remained true to the cause for which they had come.  John Winthrop sent a ship back to England for food and more colonists.  The ship returned in February 1631, in time to save the starving colony.  For several years the new settlers were severely tested by more than the ordinary sufferings of frontier life.  Only their great faith in God enabled these people to survive.  It was necessary to carry guns for protection from the Native warriors, even on the way to church, where they met to hear the same famous ministers who had once preached to them in some of England’s loveliest churches.  Now they shivered in barn-like meeting houses as they gave attention to the words of the great scholars who were their leaders.  The fine clothing, they loved; silk hose, crocked hats, and gaily colored silk, satin, and velvet dresses, cloaks, and breeches were out of place in the tangles of the New England’s forests.  Men of the “gentry class”; former justices of English counties, members of the English Parliament, merchants, artisans, and shopkeepers now all labored with their hands to wrest a bare living from this harsh new land they were now living in.  Wolves killed precious young calves.  Women and children, and sometimes even men got lost in the woods and froze to death.  Others drowned in storms out at sea while out fishing.

In 1630, Thomas French Sr., his wife Susan (Riddlesdale) and their seven children, including twenty-two-year-old son Thomas Jr. sail from England on the ship “Arabella” as part of the Winthrop Fleet and settled in the Salem community in the Massachusetts Bay Colony.

In 1630, Thomas French Jr. and Mary Scudamore were married in Boston shortly after Thomas arrived with his parents and siblings from England.

In 1630, Dr. Richard Palgrave, his wife Anne (unknown) and their four children, including nine-year-old daughter Sarah Palgrave sailed from England as part of the Winthrop fleet and arrived and settled in Charlestown, in the Massachusetts Bay Colony.  Dr. Richard Palgrave was the first physician in Charlestown and was granted 200 acres in the area, which later became part of the town of Woburn north of Charlestown on which Richard farmed.  Dr. Palgrave was made a “freeman” on 18 May 1631 in Charlestown.

In 1630, Thomas Lamb, his first wife Elizabeth (Smith) and their children sailed from England with the Winthrop fleet and arrived in the Massachusetts Bay Colony.  Thomas owned a home lot of eighteen acres and thirty-seven acres of meadow and marsh lands. Thomas Lamb opened and operated the first rock quarry on Squantum Island in Boston Harbor.  The stones were used extensively for cemetery purposes and as material for buildings.  On 18 May 1631 Thomas was made a “freeman”.

In 1630, George Alcock, his wife Anne (Hooker) and their 4-year-old son John Douglas Alcock left England and sailed to Roxbury in the Massachusetts Bay Colony as a member of the early planters of the Winthrop fleet.  George worked as a “butcher” in Roxbury.  He was admitted as the 51st member to the Roxbury church.  In 1631, George was made a “freeman”.  The towns of Roxbury and Boston were only three miles apart.

In 1630, Thomas Richardson and his two brothers Samuel and Ezekiel sailed with the Winthrop fleet and arrived and settled in Charlestown, Suffolk Co., Massachusetts Bay Colony.  Thomas was the youngest of the three brothers.

In 1630, Thomas Green Jr. was born to Thomas Green Sr. and Elizabeth (Lynde) in Kegworth, Leicestershire, England.

In 1630, Roger Williams was a chaplain in the Church of England, who believed in religious freedom, tolerance and the separation between church and state, however, he did not believe in forcing others to agree with him.  He thought that it was a sin to punish or kill anyone in the name of Christianity and he thought that only church members should pay to support their church.  On 5 Feb 1631, he and his wife arrived on the ship the “Lyon” and settled the Nantasket settlement in the Massachusetts Bay Colony.  Roger was offered serve in the church of Boston while the Rev. John Wilson traveled to England to bring his wife over to Boston.  Roger declined the offer and began speaking and writing about his ideas.  He wrote a book saying it was wrong to punish people for having different beliefs.  Then he said that the European settlers were stealing the Native Americans' land.  He said the king of England had no right to permit people to settle on land that was not his.

In 1630, Sir Richard Saltonstall paddled up the Charles River from the town of Charlestown and established the site of the new Saltonstall Plantation, one of the earliest settlements in the Massachusetts Bay Colony.  The site had been inhabited for thousands of years before the arrival of English settlers.  The Native American tribes; the “Massachusetts”, the “Pequossette” and the “Nonantum”, had settlements communities on the banks of the Charles River.  The Pequossette people built a fishing weir to trap the annual spring migration of both alewife and blueback herring fish that swim upstream from the ocean to spawn in fresh water.  The new Saltonstall plantation was officially incorporated a year later as “Waterton” that later was renamed “Watertown”.  The new settlement quickly grew and its first gristmill was installed in 1632.  Watertown was the chief horse and cattle raising market in New England and was known for its fertile gardens and fine estates.  In 1632, the residents of Watertown refused to pay a tax to support the new settlement of Cambridge that was located 5 miles east because the tax was levied on them without their permission.  Thus, the cry of “No taxation without representation” originated there.

About 1630, Hugh Maxwell was born to Edwin Hugh Maxwell and Marian (Anderson) in Airth, Stirlingshire, Scotland.

On 12 Jun 1630, the fleet’ flagship the “Arabella” of the Winthrop fleet was investigating the region around the earlier Cape Ann settlement and met and entertained, the Native chief of the Agawam Native tribe, Chief Masconomet.  The event was recorded in John Winthrop's journal.  The newly arrived Puritans spent the first winter in Charlestown where previous English settlers were living.  That first winter hundreds of the new Puritan colonists died from malnutrition and disease.  They also experienced their first “nor’easter” storm,  which cost them some fingers and toes, and many of their houses were destroyed in fires.  On 16 Sep 1630, just as John Winthrop was handing out the last handfuls of their grain supply to the famished settlers the ship “Lyon” from England entered Boston Harbor with fresh supplies of much needed food for the starving Puritans living in Charleston in the Massachusetts Bay Colony.  John Winthrop Sr. had sent for his family back in England and his wife Margaret their children, including eldest son, John Jr. from John Winthrop’s first wife, Mary Forth, arrived back on the rescue ship the “Lyon”

In Jun 1636, Ruth Wood was born to unknown parents in England.  At a later date, Ruth Wood became a “child ward” along with Mary Grove of Ralph Mousall and his wife Alice and their family in the town of Charlestown in the Massachusetts Bay Colony.  Ralph Mousall and his wife Alice and their two sons had arrived in the town of Charlestown in 1630 from England.  Ralph Mousall was a church Deacon of the Charleston and a successful farmer in Charleston.  The English process of giving children of poor families to other successful families was called “binding out”.  Ruth Wood fits the patten of a “binding out” child.

In the 1630’s, a series of European disease epidemics occurred, which severely reduced the number of Native people living in and around the Connecticut River Valley and a power vacuum resulted.  The Pequot Native tribe’s territory was the southeastern area of what today is the State of Connecticut.  The Pequot’s, with support of the Dutch fur traders, tried to prevent other tribes from trading with the English fur trading companies.  Fighting started after English fur trader John Stone and a member of his crew were murdered by the Pequots in the summer of 1634.  The Pequots admitted killing the traders, but defended their actions as a retribution (payback) for the killing on one of their people by the Dutch.  The Pequot viewed the English and Dutch as the same outside intruders on their land.

Note:  A book “Edmund Rice and his Family” by Elsie Hawes Smith describes the life Edmund Rice and Thomasine (Frost) faced when they arrived in America.  King Charles I, had recently become king of England.  The new king was viewed as selfish and ill-advised and made life for the English an unhappy experience for most people.  Taxes were high and opportunities to better one’s self were practically non-existent.  A few of the more adventurist souls looking for better opportunities chose to take a long hazardous voyage to the New World hoping for a new start and a better life.  Edmund Rice, wife Thomasine and their children set sail in 1638 for the Massachusetts Bay Colony.  The ship they sailed on had limited space for baggage and Edmund’s family was limited to only taking a few head of cattle, goats and some farm tools.  They also brought guns and ammunition and seeds for growing crops.  Thomasine brought a carved chest filled with blankets, linens, extra clothing and utensils.  They arrived with other families with similar belongings ready to make their new home in America.  Most of their home family furniture had to be left in England. Note:  On 13 Dec 1636, the National Guard started when the General Court of the Massachusetts Bay Colony ordered the colony's militia companies into three regiments: the North, South and East Regiments.  The colonists had adopted the English militia system which obligated all males, between the ages of 16 and 60, to possess arms and participate in the defense of the community.  The early colonial militias drilled once a week and provided guardsmen for details each evening to stand watch and sound the alarm in the event of an attack.

Between Jul 1636 and Sep 1638, the “Pequot War” occurred when various Native American tribes fought for control of beaver fur pelts trading business in the New England area.  In the 1630s, the whole Connecticut River Valley region was in an uproar.  The Pequot Native tribe aggressively extended its control over the Connecticut River Valley at the expense of the Wampanoag Native tribe that lived north of the valley, the Narragansett Native tribe that lived to the east, Mohegan Native tribe that lived to the west and the Algonquian Native tribe that lived in Connecticut River Valley itself.  The Lenape Algonquian Native people that lived out on Long Island were also impacted.  The Pequot Native tribe aligned itself with the Dutch and its fur trading company against English colonists and their Mohegans tribe allies for control of the beaver fur trading business with European fur buyers.

In Jul 1636, another English fur trader John Oldham was murder by Narragansett warriors who were allied with the Pequot tribe.  News of John Oldham's death became the subject of sermons in the Massachusetts Bay Colony.  In Aug 1636, the Massachusetts Bay Colony’s Governor Vane sent John Endecott, with 90 militia soldiers, to exact revenge on the Pequot Natives for the killings and a small scrimmage occurred with the Pequots on Block Island, off the coast of the Rhode Island Colony, however the Pequot warriors and their families were able to escape the attack by leaving on their boats.  The Massachusetts Bay militia burned the Pequot village on the island to the ground.  They also carried away the Pequot harvested crops, which had been stored in the island for the upcoming winter.  John Endecott and his soldiers next continued on to Fort Saybrook on the Atlantic coast where the Connecticut River flows into the ocean, where he acquired additional English colonists’ soldiers familiar with the area as guides.  John Endecott’s military militia then sailed west along the coast to a Pequot village, where he repeated the previous year's demand for identification of those responsible for the death of John Stone, and now also for those who murdered John Oldham.  After some discussion, John Endecott concluded that the Pequots were stalling and attacked their village, but most of the Pequots were able to escaped into the woods.  John Endecott again had his soldiers burn down the Pequot’s village and took their stored crops before sailing home.

The Pequots attempted to get other tribes to join them in the fight with English colonists, but were only partly successful in getting their traditional enemies to help, and the Mohegan and the Narragansett tribes openly sided with the English colonists.  The Narragansetts had lost the fur trapping territory to the Pequots back in 1622.  Through the autumn and winter of 1636, Fort Saybrook was effectively besieged by the Pequots warriors.  When spring arrived in 1637, the Pequots stepped up their raids on Connecticut towns.  They attacked a number of English towns killing men, women, cattle and horses, and took two young girls’ captive.  (The girls were daughters of William Swaine and were ransomed back by Dutch traders.)  In all, the English Connecticut towns lost about thirty settlers.

The new Connecticut colonists used the Massachusetts' “civil and ecclesiastical laws” to set up their initial government but discarded the requirement that only members of approved churches could become “freemen”.  Most people who came to the American colonies came as indentured servants.  According to English law, it was only after a man had paid or worked off his indentured servant contract that he could apply to become a member of the church and own lands.  In Connecticut whether a man was indentured servant or not, if he entered a colony as a free person and successfully completed a 1-to-2-years probationary period to make sure he was an upright Puritan, he would be accepted as a “freeman” in Connecticut.  If he failed during his probationary period, he could be forced to leave the colony.  Only 229 men were admitted as “freemen” in Connecticut between 1639 and 1662.  Rev. In May 1637, leaders of Connecticut River towns met in the town of Hartford and raised a new militia, and placed Captain John Mason in command.  Captain Mason set out with ninety militiamen and seventy Mohegan warriors to attack the Pequots at their fort.  At Fort Saybrook, Captain Mason was joined by John Underhill with another twenty militiamen.  John Underhill and Captain Mason then sailed east from Fort Saybrook to Narragansett Bay in Rhoda Island, a tactic intended to mislead Pequot spies along the shoreline into thinking that the English were not intending an attack.  After gaining the support of an additional 200 Narragansett warriors, Captain Mason and John Underhill marched their combined forces approximately twenty miles towards Pequot stronghold at Fort Mystic.  They briefly camped at Porter's Rocks near the head of the Mystic River before mounting a surprise attack just before dawn.  See a map on the next page showing the major events of the Pequot War:

The “Mystic Massacre” started in the pre-dawn hours of 26 May 1637, when colonial forces, along with their Mohegan and Narragansett warriors, surrounded one of two main fortified Pequot villages at Mystic.  Twenty militiamen successfully breached the Pequot fortified village’s palisade's gate, but they were quickly overwhelmed and had set a fire to create chaos to facilitate their escape.  The majority of the Pequots who managed to escape the spreading fire were soon slain by the colonial soldiers and their supporting Native warriors who quickly surrounded the fortified village.  An estimated 500 Pequots were killing in the fighting and the fire and only seven were taken prisoner and another seven escaped to the woods.

The destruction of Native Pequot people and their villages at Mystic Fort and the additional losses that occurred during the withdrawal pursuit broke the remaining roughly 400 Pequot warriors’ spirit, and they decided to abandon their villages and flee westward to seek refuge with the Mohawk Native tribe.  In mid-Jun 1637, Captain John Mason next set out from the town of Saybrook with 160 colonial militia solders and 40 Mohegan warrior scouts and caught up with the Pequot refugees at village near present-day Fairfield Connecticut.  The Colonial militia force surrounded the Pequot village allowed several hundred Pequot to surrender, mostly were women and children, but before dawn eighty Pequot warriors escaped and continued west.  Sassacus, who was the leader of the escaped Pequot War Party, hoped to gain refuge among the Mohawk in present-day New York.  However, the Mohawk instead murdered him and his bodyguard and sent his head and hands back to Hartford Connecticut (for reasons which were never made clear).  The Pequot War finally ended, but colonial officials continued to call for hunting down the remaining Pequots warriors after war's end.  Mind were changed and asylum was granted to any Pequots who went to live with the Narragansetts or Mohegans tribes.

Note:  In 1637, the smallpox disease arrived in the Native Huron village.  Smallpox was the most destructive disease brought to North American by Europeans.  There were a variety of infectious diseases existing in North America prior to the arrival of Christopher Columbus, however the fact that the native people were a single race, limited in total number and there was only a small number of domestic animals living with the Native people, which could carry diseases, had limited the Native population contact with major disease epidemics.  The fact that there was also a limited interactions between the Native American native populations (as compared to areas of Europe and Asia (Eurasia), help limit the spread in disease in North America.  Eurasia (Europe and Asia) on the other hand was a crossroads among many distant, different races of peoples separated by hundreds and sometimes thousands of miles.  Repeated warfare by invading populations and trade between different distant groups of people throughout the Eurasia lead to a wide spread infectious diseases.  As a result of chronic exposure to the various diseases, the of people in Eurasia who survived gradually developed natural immunity (protection) for the diseases.  The early Europeans that came to the New World carried their deadly endemic diseases with them, which killed an estimated 80 to 90% of the North American native people . This impact of the deadly Old-World diseases on the people in the New World people was inevitable, if it had not come from Europe, it would have come from Asia.  The New World could not have stay isolated once sea going ship were developed.

Note:  The Sillery settlement was named for Noël Brûlart de Sillery, a famous Hospitaller knight who died in 1640. A community called “Sillery” was established at a cove in the St Lawrence River where the Algonquin native people gathered in the summer to catch spawning eels and salmons.  Forty Algonquin Christian families move into the new settlement where they lived most of the year, except for the fall hunting season.  The new settlement residents included Algonquins from as far away as the Ottawa River Valley.  French Missionaries came to Sillery to study with the indigenous resident people to learn their languages before going to more distant settlements.  By 1640, the Augustinian Catholic sisters had established a convent in the Sillery community, which hosted non-permanent residents of the community, especially refugees of the “Beaver War”.  However, the Sillery native indigenous community failed when of the community's native people fell victims to European introduced infectious diseases like smallpox.

In 1645, the “Parliamentarians Army” led by Puritan Oliver Cromwell, referred to as his “New Model Army”’ defeated the English Royalist Army of King Charles I at the “Battle of Naseby” ending the “English Civil War”. On 8 Nov 1651, David Stirling, the father of William Sterling and his brother John Stirling sailed from Gravesend, the port of London, on the ship “John and Sarah” to the Massachusetts Bay Colony.  The two Stirling brothers were part of 272 Scottish prisoners captured in the English Civil War. On 20 Oct 1651, the English Parliament freed the imprisoned Scottish soldiers and had shipped to the English American Colonies as “indentured servants”.  Having been a supporter of the dethroned King Charles I, the Stirling family must have been Catholics.  The success the family had in melding in with the New England Puritan society indicates they were willing to do more than just change the spelling of their last name to fit in with the other protestant New English settlers in the colony.

In 1651, the village of Littleton in the Massachusetts Bay Colony was one of the original “Praying Indian Villages”.  Rev. John Eliot, known as the “Apostle to the Indians”, began an effort to organize the Massachusetts’ Native American people into Christian Villages.  With the backing of Oliver Cromwell and £12,000 sterling given him by the English government, John Eliot began a long-term mission to translate the Bible into the Algonquin language.  Although he was a Puritan, Eliot was also a humanitarian and he felt that the best way to assure the Native American peoples’ survival in the midst of heavy English land-pressure was to organize the Native Americans into English towns and English lifestyles.  The Native people were to convert to Christianity, receive deeds to their property in new towns, live in English houses, wear English clothes, and worship Puritan style in church meeting houses.  Between 1651 and 1658, John Eliot and his assistant Daniel Gookin organized seven Praying Indian Villages in Massachusetts Bay Colony.  All told there were over 14 such villages established in Massachusetts Bay Colony each with between 45 to 60 Native inhabitants.

Rev. John Eliot allowed the Native people to choose the sites for their new villages.  The local Concord Native people led by an early Christian convert, Chief Sachem Tahattawan, requested the "Nashoba" lands close by for their new praying village.  The new Native Plantation of Nashoba was formally granted by Massachusetts Bay Colony General Court in 1651 and was laid out in rectangle 3 x 4 miles.  The area encompassed most of current day Littleton and a portion of Boxborough in Massachusetts.  The Nashoba Praying Village thrived until “King Phillip’s War” in 1675-76, when the Nashoba Native people were rounded up and dumped on Deer Island in the middle of Boston Harbor to freeze and starve.  More on the “King Phillip’s War” will be covered later.  Only a handful of the Native Nashoba people survived the ordeal and were able to return to Nashoba Village.  The Nashoba’s Plantation land was sold to English settlers and by 1714 it was completely in English settlers’ hands and re-incorporated as the Town of Littleton in 1715.  The surviving Native Nashoba people were given a new Native town on a 500-acre tract of rocky hills between Nagog Pond and Fort Pond (which was named after the Native fort located in the area).

YEAR 1653 (17 paras)

On 12 Aug 1653, William Peacock and Mary Willis were married in Roxbury, Massachusetts Bay Colony.  William Peacock was working as a “trader” with the Native Americans living in the Massachusetts Bay area.

On 23 May 1653, Pierre Gaudin, who was living in Châtillon-sur-Seine, Bourgogne-Franche-Comté, France, when he enlisted to go to Canada with several companions. On 15 Nov 1653, Jean de Lauzon, the Governor of Canada, turned over to Noël Morin a quarter-league (.8 miles) of frontal property by a league deep (3.5 miles), beginning an arpent below the La Caille River that flowed into the St. Lawrence River.  Thus, Seigneur Noël Morin became the owner of a portion of the seigneury of La Rivière-du-Sud, today part of the town of Montmagny, Québec.  Noël’s acquisition of the large area of prime land made him a fief (owner of a landed estate held on condition of feudal service) coming with rights and duties.  Noël was required to render faith and homage to the “West Indies Company”.  Noël named his new domain “Saint-Luc”.  Noël and wife Hélène (Desportes) never moved out to the Saint-Luc, preferring to stay and live in Québec settlement.  Their large Saint-Luc estate was later divided among their sons and son-in-law Guillaume Fournier.

In 1653, Jean-Baptiste, the first mayor of the Québec settlement, concedes to Pierre Maheu land of 1.7 acres on of frontage on the Lièver River one mile wide and half a mile depth in L'Ange-Gardien, which was located 10 miles east of Québec settlement.  Pierre worked as a “weaver of clothing” in the L'Ange-Gardien settlement.

According to family tradition Paul arrived with gold coins sewn into the lining of his coat.  Paul was a “mason” by trade and gained the necessary skills to become a “seigneurial notary”(seigneurial attorney).

On 22 Oct 1653, Paul Vachon and Marguerite Langlois were married in Beauport, Québec, Canada.  Paul Vachon was working in Beauport as a notary and clerk to the area court.

On 16 Nov 1653, Governor Paul Chomedey and his one-hundred new Frenchmen recruits arrive in the Ville-Marie settlement essentially guaranteeing the survival of the small remote settlement.

On 17 Nov 1653, thirty-two-year-old Nicolas Gaudry and twelve-year-old Agnés Morin were married in the Québec settlement in Canada.  (There was a real scarcity of marriageable women in Canada at this time.)

In 1653, Toussaint Giroux left Mortagne, Perche, France and sailed to the Québec settlement in Canada.  Toussaint had signed a contract with Robert Gillard to cover the cost of the voyage to Canada by working for three years as an “ell fisherman” on the St. Lawrence River and as a “house servant” for Robert Gillard.

On 15 Nov 1653, Noël Morin was granted a small “arriere fief” (French for tenant in chief) over his land, located within the larger Rivière-du-Sud seigneurie on the south shore of the St. Lawrence River at Montmagny.  Noël was able to rent out his land to earn an income.  Noël Morin was known as “Sieur de Saint-Luc”.  As a seigneur Noël Morin and his wife Hélène (Desportes) were honored in a number of ways.  The social life of the seigneuries, as well as the colony itself, was centered around the Catholic church.  For this reason, many of the honors paid to a seigneur were accorded through the church.  The seigneur was granted a special pew in the church that was larger than the rest of the other pews and located in the most prominent place in the church, the front row.  Noël and his family would have been mentioned by name in the prayers from the priests in the church pulpit.  In various church ceremonies the seigneur was positioned at the head of the line and upon his death was buried inside the church beneath his seigneur pew.

On 16 Nov 1653, Jacques-Hugues Picard arrived in the Ville Marie settlement as part of the “Grande Rescue” French military force.  Jacques-Hugues was skilled in clearing land and sawing lumber as a “top sawyer”.

In 1653, thirteen-year-old Michel Messier, a young French solder, arrived in the Ville Marie settlement as one of the 102 settlers that were members of the “Grande Rescue”.  The Iroquois attacks continued, and Michel Messier was captured in the fall of 1654.  The next summer, a Mohawk chief returned Michel and other captured Frenchmen to the Ville Marie settlement in exchange for Iroquois chiefs that were being held by the French.

In 1653, François Bélanger was the mayor of the Longue Pointe in Canada, which later became the parish of Saint-Anne-de-Beaupré.

In 1653, Jean Bonneau was a member of the Huguenot French Protestants that were being persecuted by the Catholic leaders of France.  Jean Bonneau left his wife Catherine (Poulet) and grown children in Mesnil, Picardie, France and sailed to Canada.  Jean Bonneau was the first of the Bonneau/Goodwater branch of Winifred Eloise Coss’ family tree.

In 1653 Catherine Hurault arrived alone in the Montréal settlement as a thirteen-year-old “filles à marier” girl.

In 1653, twenty-five-year-old Jean Lemarché arrived in the Montréal settlement as a soldier as part of the 102 members of the “Grande Rescue”.

The Benjamin Bonneau III’s family fit the profile of poor subsistence Canadian farmers looking for a better life.  The Bonneau family starting in Canada when Jean Bonneau’s arrival in the Ville Marie settlement back in 1653, where he farmed for a living.  Jean’s son Pierre and his family followed his father to Canada a year later.  Pierre’s son Joseph had an opportunity to acquire land on the Île d’Orléans Island when the area opened up to settlers.  Joseph’s son Dominique Sr. was the 5th son of Joseph’s second marriage and one of Joseph’s twenty-two children. YEAR 1655 (13 paras)
============================================================

In May 1655, the Massachusetts Bay Colony’s General Court approved the creation of a new community called Chelmsford near the towns of Concord and Woburn in the colony.  The settlement of Chelmsford was a horrible example of taking land from the Native American people.  The Pennacook Native people (meaning bottom of the hill) had lived in the area selected for the new Chelmsford community for thousands of years.  The Native Americans were Algonquian-speaking people who operated as a network of politically and culturally allied communities rather than a single large tribe.  The major Pennacook villages were built along the rivers in the area.  Life revolved around the seasons with Native women beginning in the spring collecting maple sap and making maple sugar.  Men would burn the forests to add nutrients in the soils for later cultivation.  In late spring the rivers and creeks would swell as the salmon and shad fish making their way up the rivers to spawn.  Many Pennacook villages were built above natural waterfalls that gathered the spawning fish and made it easier to catch them in the late spring.  The delicious “fiddlehead fern” season occurred next followed by other seasons known today, like blueberry and the raspberry seasons.  During the summers, the Native families would disperse out to summer villages and hunting camps.  Women did most of the work of building and maintaining homes as well as farming.  Their main crops were varieties of corn and squash, which they planted along rivers and in meadows.  While they found it difficult to clear the massive old-growth forest trees, the Pennacook’s were experts at manipulating beavers to move their dams and build ponds up and down creeks and brooks, thereby clearing and opening up land for farms that would be essential to the first Europeans settlers who arrived and found cleared fields ready for cultivation.

Between 1655 and 1662, the inhabitants of Trois Rivières settlement, when not fighting off Iroquois attacks, were a quarrelsome bunch.  In the "Prévôté Court de Trois-Rivières" there were 907 cases tried for a population of about 700 settlers living in Trois Rivières.  The "Prévôté Court” was not only used to dispense criminal justice and adjudicate disputes, it also served as a collection agency under the settlement’s barter economy.  66% of the court cases handled in the "Prévôté Court” were for debt settlement and 20% for settling inheritance claims.  Only 20 were for verbal or physical violence.  Lt. Quentin Moral was involved in 29 cases, reflecting not only his role as an attorney but also his quarrelsome nature and perhaps developed from his duties as an officer of the King.  In one case, Quentin was being sued for having shot and killed other citizen’s wandering pigs, probably part of his duties.  A few cases later, Quentin sued Jacques Aubuchon, a master carpenter and the most quarrelsome man in the colony (44 cases), because the Jacques had intentionally shot one of Quentin's pigs, (perhaps as payback?).

In return for the privilege of having their own pew in the church, Noël’s paid the “Fabrique Corporation”, 1.7 acres of land, which today is the location of the Cathedral-Basilica of Notre-Dame de Québec ("Our Lady of Quebec City"), located at 16, Rue de Buade street in Quebec City, which is the “primatial church” (where the church bishop resides) of the Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Quebec.
