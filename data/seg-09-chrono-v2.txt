YEAR 1818 (2 paras)
============================================================
On 20 Oct 1818 , the “Convention respecting fiseries, boundary and the restoration of slaves” or the “Treaty of 1818” was signed between the United States and the United Kingdom.  This treaty resolved standing boundary issues in North America between the two nations.  The treaty allowed for joint occupation and settlement of the Oregon Territory.  The two nations agreed to a boundary line at the 49th parallel north separating the United States and Canada, in part because a straight-line boundary would be easier to survey than the pre-existing boundaries based on watersheds of rivers.  The Great Britain ceded all of Rupert’s Land south of the 49th parallel and east of the Continental Divide, including all of the Red River Colony south of the 49 parallel, while the United States ceded the northernmost edge of the Missouri Territory north of the 49th parallel.  See a map below showing the new 49th boundary line between Canada and the United States:

In 1818, a group of French missionaries living in a Roman Catholic mission in the Red River Colony settlement, worked with the Ojibwe and Cree Native people in the Red River area to introduce them to Christianity, particularly the 72 native women who had married French Canadian men and were currently living in the settlement area.  By 1823, there were about 350 mixed French/Native American families called “Métis” families living in and around Red River Colony settlement and another 450 “Métis” families living in the present North Dakota area.  “Métis” was a French word developed back in the early fur trading period to identify people of mixed European and Indigenous Native parents.

YEAR 1850 (5 paras)
============================================================
Note:  The French family name of Bonneau was perverted to Bono (easer to say and spell) when Benjamin Bonneau III was living in Québec City.  The French name Bonneau translates to “Good water” in English and Benjamin adopted the English translation of his name, and it appeared as Benjamin’s last name on the 1850 Census in Wheelock, Caledonia Co., Vermont.  Mrs. Delina Bonneau wife of Vitral Bonneau, not related to Benjamin, had also used Goodwater as an alias name when she completed her request in 1880 for U.S. Civil Was Pension upon the death of her husband, indicating other Bonneau’s also ran into issues with the Bonneau French name.

In the 1850 U. S. Census, Warren Coss and wife Edith (Powers) and their six children were living in Palmyra, Jefferson Co., Wisconsin.  Warren was farming on his $400 valued farmland.  The fact that the census included the value on the land Warren and Edith were living on indicates they owned the land.  The censuses prior to 1850 did not include land value estimates so land ownership was not possible to determine, except in the probate process, which occurred upon death in “will” distributions.  Warren Coss’ twenty-three son Joseph Warren Coss was no longer living with the Warren family.  At some point prior to 1850, Joseph Warren Coss had left the family and moved out 190 miles farther west from Palmyra Wisconsin to Winneshiek County, Iowa.

In the 1850 U. S. Census, Benjamin Goodwater III and wife Mary Louise (Carriere) and their five children, including eleven-year-old daughter Mary Ann Goodwater were living in Wheelock, Caledonia Co., Vermont.  Benjamin was farming and had changed his family’s last name from Bonneau to Goodwater.

After 12 Sep 1850, when the 1850 U. S. Census for Benjamin Goodwater III and his family was recorded, the family was now living in Ossian, Winneshiek Co., Iowa.  Benjamin and his family had left Wheelock, Caledonia Co., Vermont and traveled an incredible 1,300 miles west to Iowa.  The new United States railroad networks were underway by 1850 and ships were crossing Lake Erie at the time.  The family probably used multiple modes of transportation starting with the Erie Canal that would take them to from Albany New York to Buffalo New York on Lake Erie.  Crossing Lake Erie by boat would have taken them to Toledo, Ohio.  Railroads would next take them from Toledo out west of Chicago to Rockford Illinois where the railroad ended, leaving the family 200 miles from their Iowa destination.

As new lands became available in Iowa, Minnesota and Wisconsin the 1850s, they were quickly claimed by new Euro-American settlers that initially used the land for “subsistence farming”, the growing and raising the food needed to allow the family to survive.  By 1860 most of the land in the three states was all claimed.  The American Civil War disrupted the “subsistence farming” system as men were called up to serve in the U.S. Army.  Between 1850 and 1860 new railroad lines were constructed in the three states that change the farming process from primarily one of feeding the farm family to “commodity farming” were farm products were grown and raised to be sold for a profit.  How well the settlers’ families adopted the new entrepreneurial farming opportunity resulted in some successful farms growing in size and the less successful farming families’ fathers and sons having to work on the more successful farms as farmworkers for wages.

In 1851, the first “Treaty of Fort Laramie” was signed in an attempted to resolve disputes between Native American tribes and the U. S. Government, as well as among individual Native tribes themselves, in areas that will become the states of Montana, Wyoming, Nebraska, North Dakota and South Dakota.  The treaty specified that the tribes would make peace among one another and allow outside access to their Native lands for activities such as travelling, surveying, and the construction of government outposts and roads.  As part of the treaty the individual Native tribes would be responsible for wrongs committed by their own people.  In return, the U. S. Government agreed to protect the Native tribes on their own land and pay an annual of $50,000 annuity each year to the Native tribes.

In the 1861 English Census, forty-two-year-old Edward Collingwood and his thirty-six-year-old wife Jane Collingwood (Banks) with their seventeen-year-old daughter Mary Collingwood, fifteen-year-old daughter Jane, thirteen-year-old daughter Margret and one-year-old son John Thomas were listed as living in the home in Cornforth, Durham, United Kingdom.  Mary Collingwood birth place was listed as Shadforth, Durham, England.  The village of Shadforth is located less than that a mile east of the village of Running Waters, where the 1851 English Census listed Mary Collingwood was born.  Additionally, Mary Collingwood was baptized by her parents in Shadforth.  Widower Edward Collingwood had remarried Jane Banks in 1858 after losing his first wife Sarah Gary and was farming on 35 acres of land for a living.

On 17 Aug 1862, the “Dakota War”, also known as the “Sioux Uprising” started along the Minnesota River, after the U. S. Federal Government failed to deliver the annuity payments that had been promised to Sioux Nation back in the 1851 in the “Treaty of Fort Laramie”.  The Santee Sioux warriors reacted by pillaging the village of New Ulm in Brown Co. Minnesota and attacked Fort Ridgely located on a hill overlooking the Minnesota River southwest of Fairfax, Minnesota.  The Sioux warriors killed over 800 German farmers, including men, women and children.  The Sioux Nation was eventually defeated on 23 Sep 1862 in the “Battle of Wood Lake” in Yellow Medicine Co., Minnesota.

Over the next two years, other Sioux warriors continued to attack settlers in the Dakota Territory in a series of large raids.  The final two battles were fought in July 1864 in Dunn County, Dakota Territory and in Aug 1864, in the Dakota Territory’s western badlands on the border with Montana near the present town of Medora, North Dakota.  The U. S. Army, which was deeply involved in the “American Civil War” at the time in 1864, spared some soldiers and managed to force the surrender of most of the Dakota tribal bands by the end of Dec 1864.  The U. S. Congress next passed legislation that abolished the Native America reservations in the Mankato area, which resulted in the loss of the ten-mile-wide strip of land on each side of the Minnesota River the Sioux Nation had retained when the ceded most of their land back in 1851.  With the loss of the last southern Minnesota land held by the Sioux Nation tribe, new rich farmland became available for settlers.  The “Sioux Uprising” occurred because to the broken promises that were made to the tribe by the United States government and the horrible living conditions the Sioux people were forced accept on their previous ancestral homeland.  Their treatment was very unfair and quickened the almost total demise of the Sioux Native American people.
