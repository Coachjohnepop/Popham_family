YEAR 1862 (5 paras)
============================================================
On 20 May 1862 President Abraham Lincoln signed the “Homestead Act”.  The act granted the U. S. Federal Government more than 270 million acres of land for homesteading.  To make a homestead claim, a potential homesteader had to pay a filing fee of $10 to make a temporary claim on the land, plus $2 for commission to the land agent and an additional $6 final payment to receive an official patent on the land.  Land titles could also be purchased from the government for $1.25 per acre following six months proven residency.  See maps below showing the lands that were taken from the Native American people and was opened to settlers:

In 1862, to help the United States fund the Civil War the U.S. Congress passed the “Legal Tender Act”, authorizing the printing of $150 million in new U.S. paper money, colored in distinctive green color on the backside, which was quickly nicknamed  the “Greenback”.  The “Legal Tender Act” was followed up by Congress creating a National Banking System, which was  authorized to issue banknotes backed by the U.S. Government.  Government Reserve Banks were next formed in the country’s largest cities holding money reserves to backup local banks established in the country’s smaller towns.  With a new banking system in place settler families were able now to borrow money to buy homestead farming property.  If the settler farmer family was unable to grow or raise extra farming products they could sell, there was little chance they could pay off their borrowed bank loan.

Most of the Sioux warriors who took part in the fighting at “Battle of Wood Lake” escaped into the “Dakota Territory” and continue to attack settlers.  303 Santee Sioux Nation warriors finally surrendered on 26 Sep 1862 at Camp Release to the U. S. Army.  In the Military murder trials that followed and all 303 Santee Sioux warriors were found guilty of killing settlers and sentenced to death.  After a closer review of the 303 of the death sentences all but 38 were overturned by the U. S. Federal government and only the 38 of the warriors’ leaders remained guilty and were all hanged to death on 26 Dec 1862 in the Town of Mankato, Minnesota in United States’ largest mass-execution.

The fact that her parents were not married cause last name confusion for Sarah Mary who was listed with the last name “Pearson” in some documents and “Collingwood” in other documents and also listed with both last names or no last name in even other documents.

Note:  Back on 14 Jul 1862, the U.S. Congress passed the “An Act to grant Pensions “.  The act awarded pensions to injured soldiers in the Civil War and to surviving widows and minor children of soldiers killed in the war.  In 1890 the “Disability Act of 1890” was passed that removed the restriction of war-related injuries, allowing pensions to be awarded to solders solely on time of service in the Civil War.

On 16 Feb 1865, Private Joseph Warren Coss enlisted in Company M in the 1st Minnesota Heavy Artillery Regiment as a thirty-eight-year-old private, with blue eyes, brown hair and being 5”9” tall.  The 1st Minnesota Heavy Artillery Regiment had organized on 1 Sep 1864 and mustered out (sent to war) on 27 Sep 1865.  The regiment was commanded by Colonel William Colville who was ordered by Major General Ulysses S. Grant to reenforce, protect and control the vital railroad junction in Chattanooga, Tennessee during the winter of 1864/65.  Chattanooga and its rail lines were critical to hold to allow supplies to flow to General William Tecumseh Sherman, who had just launched his Atlantic campaign to march across George to the port city of Savannah thus cutting the Confederacy in half.  The 1st Minnesota Heavy Artillery Regiment was composed of twelve companies, each containing 140 soldiers and officers.  The 1st Minnesota Heavy Artillery Regiment was assigned to the key heavy guns that were in place to protect Chattanooga from an expected counter-attack by General Braxton Bragg’s Confederate Army.  The Confederate Army assessed the Chattanooga defenses and never attempted an attack.  1st Minnesota Heavy Artillery Regiment, with their mission accomplished, remained in Chattanooga until the close of the “Civil War”.  Starting in June 1865 companies of the regiment began mustering out of service and by Oct 1865 the 1st Minnesota Regiment no long existed.  Private Joseph Warren Coss stayed in the regiment until the end mustering out on 27 Sep 1865.

The house had 42 rooms for visiting guests and hot and cold running water in the era of hand-pumps.  The house was fully protected from mosquitoes by netting.  Adjacent to the house was a billiards-hall; and situated nearby were “archery and tennis grounds, swings, tents and quite retreats protected by shade canopies” of top-notch quality.  There were 22 buildings in all on the Hersey farm, which included a separate dormitory and dining hall for the farm’s workers, wagon, carriage and machinery storage halls; five horse barns, a cow barn, pig barn, chicken coop, ice house, and a slaughterhouse.  The Hersey Farm raised thoroughbred Clydesdale workhorses and high-class Jersey milk cows.  There were also 100 hogs; and several hundred Plymouth Rock hens, all of prime stock.  Dudley Hersey also established a townsite near his large farm and names it after  his wife Arvilla.  Dudley Hersey died in 1900 and his wife Arvilla moved away and the magnificence farm quickly faded away.

Note:  By the end of the 1700s inoculation against smallpox had become a popular preventive process in Europe.  Yet, epidemics of smallpox continued to affect the people in Canada and Montréal City was hit particularly hard in the 1880s . The epidemic of 1885 was especially dramatic; its scale persuaded municipal authorities to make smallpox vaccination mandatory, but medical opinion was divided into pro and anti-vaccination camps, with the latter accusing the former of spreading the deadly disease.  Many in Canada refused to be vaccinated.  In Sep 1885, a riot broke out in the Montréal City and some protesters tore down pro-vaccination posters and ransacked the homes of the official medical vaccinators, attacked the city hall, the city’s pharmacies stores and the homes of municipal government magistrates.  The extent of the smallpox epidemic took 3,164 lives with 2,117 of them being children. Charles served in the 78th Battalion two and a half years, until being discharged in Winnipeg, Manitoba, Canada on 12 Jun 1919.  The fighting in the war had ended at 11am, 11 Nov 1918 with the signing of an armistice, but the official ending of the war did not occurre until 28 Jun 1919 with the signing of the “Treaty of Versailles”.

YEAR 1920 (4 paras)
============================================================
Note:  The word “Indian”, given to the people living in the New World, originated when Christopher Columbus returned to Spain after his first trip and described the indigenous people he found to King Ferdinand and Queen Isabella on the first island he visited.  Unfortunately, there is no clear record of what island in the Bahamas was the island Columbus first landed on he called San Salvador.  The island that has that name today was called Watlings Island prior to 1920.  The description given to the King Ferdinand and Queen Isabella of the indigenous people living on the island was “Los Ninos de’la’endeo” or “children of god” a term later corrupted to los nineos de la Indio” that, later became “Indian”.  The term “children of god” was used to describe the well-mannered, praiseworthiness, straight-forwardness and honesty of the indigenous people that Columbus found in the New World.  The word “Indian” was meant as a complementary term for describing the native people of the New World.
