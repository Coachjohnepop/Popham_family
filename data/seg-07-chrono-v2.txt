Note:  In 1648, the Massachusetts Bay Colony tried and executed an accused witch for the first time.  The accused was a midwife (helper of woman giving birth) named Margaret Jones from Charlestown who was hanged at Gallows Hill in Boston after she was accused of being a witch by some of her pregnant women patients.  Margaret and her husband Thomas Jones were one of the earliest settlers in the town Charlestown.  Margaret was an herbalist (used herbs and plants as medicine) and midwife who treated her sick neighbors with various teas and herbal concoctions.  People who had the power to heal also have the power to harm, and eventually some of Margaret's patients began to murmur against her.  They claimed she said if they refused to buy her medicines they would never get well.  Soon some of her neighbors began to openly accuse Margaret of witchcraft, claiming she "was found to have such a “Malignant Touch”, as many persons were taken with deafness, or vomiting, or other violent pains and sickness."  About eighty people throughout New England were accused of practicing witchcraft between 1648 and 1693. Thirteen women and two men were executed.
In 1659, twenty-two-year-old William Sterling and Elizabeth Sawtelle were married in the town of Haverhill in the Massachusetts Bay Colony.  The record of the marriage is the first record identifying William Sterling was now living in the English American Colonies.  Elizabeth Sawtelle was the first of William’s four wives.  William Sterling appears in the 1662 records of Essex County in the Massachusetts Bay Colony, where the birth of his first child was entered as born in the town of Rowley in the Massachusetts Bay Colony.  The town of Rowley was 13-miles east of Haverhill.  Between 1660 and 1664, William Sterling was taxed each year three shillings and ten pence by the town of Rowley.  William worked as a ship ”carpenter and grain miller”.  He was living in the Rowley in 1669, when he sold his property to Stephen Kent in exchange for the latter's house, barn and orchard in the town of Haverhill, on the Merrimac River, near a small stream called Little River.  William Sterling secured another twelve acres of land sometime prior to 1683 on the site where the city hall of the town of Haverhill, Massachusetts now stands, and built on the land a two-story house.  In the town of Haverhill, William was a “carpenter” by trade and built ships on the Merrimac River for the New England coastal traders.  He was a member of the “Board of Selectmen” in Haverhill.  William offered his house and land to the town as a home for the town’s minister.  He received in payment, ten acres of land on Fishing River, and the use of ten more acres for a grain mill he erected.  William was prominent in Haverhill town’s affairs, and from 1693 to 1696 he was one of the town’s “tithingman” (English Peace Officer) and also held the office of the town’s “Constable”, responsible for serving court documents, assisting at executions of writs (collection court fines) and maintaining order during court sessions

In 1693, Pierre Charles Le Sueur was sent west by Canada’s Governor General Louis de Buade, Comte de Frontenac, to establish forts around Lake Superior and work on establishing peace with local the native tribes.  Pierre had become fluent in several native languages, which was crucial to his success in trading and exploring.  In 1693, Pierre who spoke both the Sioux and Chippewa languages settled a dispute between the two warring native tribes.  Pierre founded a trading post at La Point in the Chequamegon Bay on the west side of Lake Superior, where Pierre found copper ore.  A large Native American village, known as “Chequamegon” and been established back in 1649, after the “Beaver Wars” as a refugee for Petun, Huron and Ottawa Native people fleeing to escape the Iroquois attacks.  Today the “Bad River Band Chippewa Indian Reservation” is located on the south shore of Chequamegon Bay.  In the 2010 census 6,945 Ojibwe Native people were living on the reservation.

On 7 Oct 1691, England’s new King William and Queen Mary II rechartered the Massachusetts Bay Colony into the new “Province of Massachusetts Bay”.  The new province charter allowed England to take control over the former Massachusetts Bay Colony.  A new English governor Sir William Phips was placed in charge of the new province.  The province included the Massachusetts Bay Colony, the Plymouth Colony, the Province of Maine, the islands of Martha’s Vineyards and Nantucket, Nova Scotia and New Brunswick.  Later in 1697, Nova Scotia and New Brunswick were separated from the Province of Massachusetts.  The new charter restricted religious-based laws, such as the church membership requirement to become a voter, extended religious tolerance to other Protestant denominations other than the Anglican Church of England , required oaths to be taken to the king and not the government of Massachusetts and tightened the English government’s overall control over the colony.  The Puritans in the colonies worried that their religion and themselves were once again under attack.

YEAR 1699 (2 paras)
============================================================
On 15 Jun 1699, Charles Carrier Sr. and Marie Gesseron are married in Lauzon, Lévis, Canada.
In 1699, Pierre Charles Le Sueur was with the group of French explorers that were traveling 1,200 miles up Mississippi River from Biloxi on the Gulf of Mexico, stopping to overwinter at Fort Perrot above Lake Pepin on the Mississippi River.  In the spring Pierre went upstream as far as Saint Anthony Fall (now the city of Minneapolis Minnesota).  After trading with the local Dakota tribal bands in the area, Pierre and a group of 20 men went further up the Mississippi River to an area the local native people called: "minisota", meaning "cloud reflected water".  This area was known to later explores as the St. Pierre River, which today is the Minnesota River.  Pierre and the group continued up to the Blue Earth River, where they built Fort L’Huiller (near Mankato, Minnesota), named for the chemist who declared the ore in the area to be copper.  They overwintered at Fort L’Huiller, trading for furs with merchandise they had brought for trade with the local Native tribes.  Pierre Le Sueur and the others found the open prairies full of American bison and learned to subsist (live) as the native people largely on a bison meat diet.

Note:  The French had been generally successful in establishing good relations with the Native American population starting with their first settlements in Acadia in back in 1604 and Québec in 1608.  The French sought out the native people with missionary efforts and trading posts.  The French worked to learn the native languages and customs that allowed them to effectively communicate with the native people.  The small number of French settlers compared to the large waves of English immigrants that were arriving in the English colonies made the French appear to be a much smaller threat to the native people’s way of life. Between 1640 and 1701, the French and the Iroquois Confederation were at war much of the time.

YEAR 1712 (5 paras)
============================================================
Note:  Around 1712, the “Industrial Revolution” started in Great Britain.  At this time most people Britain resided in small, rural communities where their daily existences revolved around farming.  Life was difficult, incomes were meager and malnourishment and disease were common.  People produced the bulk of their own food, clothing, furniture and tools.  Manufacturing was done in homes or small, rural shops using hand tools or simple machines.  The change started in agriculture where improved tools and early machines increased the amount of food that could be produced using less manual labor.  Most of the power used in doing farm tasks had come from human power or animal power.  Where available, water power (water wheels) and wind power (windmills) had been used to power small early factories.  In 1712 Thomas Newcomen, unveiled his steam-driven piston engine, and everything began to change.  Steam engines improved rapidly as the century advanced, and were put to greater and greater use.  More efficient and powerful engines were employed in coalmines, textile mills and dozens of other heavy industries.

The Wabanaki Confederacy was formed from the Mi’kmaq, Maliseet, Passamaquoddy and Penobscot tribes of Native Easter Algonquian speaking people.  The confederacy tribes felt they were being undermined by the English who kept trying to take their guns away.  Tensions rose in the area after English sailors killed a Native American young child, and the English colonial administrator Edmund Andros led a raid on small coastal town of Castine in northern Maine.  The English destroyed the home of Baron Jean-Vincent de Saint-Castin, who was a powerful French fur trader and soldier, living in the disputed area.  The Baron Saint-Castin had cemented a French alliance with the Wabanaki Confederacy people by marrying the daughter of the confederacy’s great Wabanaki Chief Madockawando.  The fighting between the English and the French and their Native American allies continued and by 1713 there were no English homes left in Acadia (now Nova Scotia) .  For more information in the “King Williams’ War” see page 215 for the French point of view on the war.

From 1722 to 1725, the “Dummer’s War” was fought between the British Province of Massachusetts Bay and the Wabanaki Confederation (specifically the Mi’kmag, Maliseet, Penobscot and Abenaki tribes), which was supported by French Colony of Canada.  The eastern theater of the war was fought primarily along the border between the British Province of Massachusetts Bay and the French Colony of Acadia on island of Nova Scotia.  A separate western theater of the war was fought in northern Province of Massachusetts Bay, which included New Hampshire and the Québec French Canada Colony.  The war was fought over disputes about the border between the French and English Colonies.  The 1713 “Treaty of Utrecht”, which had ended the “Queen Anne’s War” had transferred the Hudson Bay, Acadia and Newfoundland to Great Britain, but did not include the Wabanaki Confederation representatives.
In 1713, the “Queen Anne’s War” in Europe and North America ended with the signing of the “Treaty of Utrecht” in Europe.  Some terms were ambiguous in the treaty, and concerns of various Native American tribes were not included, thereby setting the stage for future conflicts.  The main result of the war was England emerged as a major imperial, military, commercial and financial power.  The French and English colonist both took advantage of the relative peace that followed the “Treaty of Utrecht” and gradually extended the western frontiers into the center of North America.  The French established a colony of Louisiana at the mouth of the Mississippi River and built forts up the Mississippi River all the way to the Great Lakes.  The alliances the French had built with the Native American tribes were neglected during this expansion period alienating (turning off) trust of the native people.  The Native Americans were divided between the attraction of the European advanced trade goods and the resentment over both the dependence the goods created and the loss of additional ancestral homeland caused by the flood of new European settlers, which upset the native people’s way of life.
In 1713, L'Acadie an area on the west side of the Richelieu River, across from Saint-Jean-sur-Richelieu in Quebec, 30 miles from the city of Montréal and about the same distance from the United States border at the head of Lake Champlain was settled by French people who were forced to leave the Islands of Nova Scotia, Newfoundland and the land on the southern shore of Hudson Bay in 1713, at the end of “Queen Anne’s War”.

Until 1763, the Hudson Bay Company (HBC) struggled with the French for control of the fur trade in southern Rupert’s Land.  In the early years, a series of naval and land battles took place on the Hudson and James Bays.  In 1713, as part of the “Treaty of Utrecht”, France acknowledged England’s claim to the Hudson Bay area.  For the next 60 years, the HBC erected posts at the mouths of all major rivers flowing into the Hudson and James Bays.  HBC’s reluctance to establish trading posts in the interior of Rupert’s Land allowed competitors to outflank the HBC by travelling into the interior to trade with Native peoples rather than waiting for them to come to the coast.  The Native trappers used this intense rivalry to extract greater returns for their furs.

Rev. John Eliot allowed the Native people to choose the sites for their new villages.  The local Concord Native people led by an early Christian convert, Chief Sachem Tahattawan, requested the "Nashoba" lands close by for their new praying village.  The new Native Plantation of Nashoba was formally granted by Massachusetts Bay Colony General Court in 1651 and was laid out in rectangle 3 x 4 miles.  The area encompassed most of current day Littleton and a portion of Boxborough in Massachusetts.  The Nashoba Praying Village thrived until “King Phillip’s War” in 1675-76, when the Nashoba Native people were rounded up and dumped on Deer Island in the middle of Boston Harbor to freeze and starve.  More on the “King Phillip’s War” will be covered later.  Only a handful of the Native Nashoba people survived the ordeal and were able to return to Nashoba Village.  The Nashoba’s Plantation land was sold to English settlers and by 1714 it was completely in English settlers’ hands and re-incorporated as the Town of Littleton in 1715.  The surviving Native Nashoba people were given a new Native town on a 500-acre tract of rocky hills between Nagog Pond and Fort Pond (which was named after the Native fort located in the area).

YEAR 1718 (4 paras)
============================================================
In 1718, the “Transportation Act” was passed by the British Parliament.  As the Industrial Revolution progressed workers began to leave the farms and move into the cities looking for jobs.  The cities quickly became over crowed and crime became a major problem.  The  new “Transportation Act” had two purposes: first to take criminals off the streets and out of the crowed jails and second to provide cheap labor for the British American Colonies.  Less serious crimes were punished by 7 years of labor in a colony.  More serious crimes were punished with 14 years to life sentences as labors in a colony.  After completion of the punishment period a pardon was given to individual and they were free to located to where every they pleased.  The punishment for returning to England before completing the punishment period was death.
Between 1718 and 1776, over 52,000 convicts and unemployed youth were transported to the American Colonies, primarily to Virginia and Maryland.  The outbreak of the American Revolution ended the process in 1776 in America.  From 1788 to 1869, more than 160,000 prisoners and unemployed youth were sent instead to the British Colony in Australia.

In 1744, the “King George’s War” started between Great Britain and its American colonies and France and its Canada colony, as the third of the “French and Indian Wars”. YEAR 1745 (4 paras)
============================================================
The “King George’s War” was fought on the frontiers between the northern British colonies and New France Canada.  Each side had Native American allies and a number of outlying colonial villages were raided by native warriors and captives were taken for ransom, or adoption by Native American tribes.  As a result of the frequent raiding Governor William Shirley ordered the construction of a chain of frontier outposts along the border between Province of Massachusetts Bay out to the border with the Province of New York and the French Canada Colony.  On 28 Nov 1745, the French with their Native American allies raided and destroyed the village of Saratoga in the Province of New York, killing or capturing more than one hundred of the inhabitants of the village.  After the attack the British abandoned all their settlements in the Province of New York north of the town of Albany.
In 1745, Governor William Shirley of the English Province of Massachusetts Bay, received important intelligence information on the weak defenses that were in place French held Nova Scotia from prisoners that had been exchanged with the French.  Governor immediately mobilized 4,000 New Englanders militia solders under the command of William Pepperell.  The New Englanders attacked and captured the import port of Louisbourg on Nova Scotia Island and mainland Acadia area from the French.  The following year France sent a fleet of 76 ships with 3,000 soldiers to retake Louisbourg, Nova Scotia and Acadia, but the fleet was devastated by a hurricane and an out brake of smallpox in the French soldiers in the French fleet that spread to the supporting French colonists and their allied Native American tribes.  The remaining French ships and soldiers were forced to returned to France.  The contact between the sick soldiers caused more deaths than all the fighting that occurred in the area.  The British set up a naval blockade of the French ports in Canada, which dramatically cut the flow of goods to the France colony and upset the trading that had been occurring with the Native American tribes.  The shortage of European goods drove the Native American tribes to switch to the Great Britain to trade their furs for European items the Native American tribes wanted.  The series of events starting with the hurricane, the spread of smallpox disease, the successful British naval blockade and the switching of sides of many of the Native American tribes set the stage of the upcoming disaster that was about to occur for the French in North America. In 1750, British North America colonies had grown to becoming a full-fledged independent country, something they would actually become a few decades later, with 1 million inhabitants.
