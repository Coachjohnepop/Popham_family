YEAR 1656 (18 paras)
============================================================

In 1651, Edward Preston and twenty-eight-year-old Margaret Hurst were married in the town of New Haven in the Connecticut Colony.  No record has been found for Margaret’s voyage from England to New England.  Margaret’s father Edward Peter Hurst had emigrated to the town of Boston in the Massachusetts Bay Colony, prior to his death in Boston on 16 Jun 1656.  Margert’s mother Susanna (Tristram) had died in 1670 back in Hitchin, Hertfordshire, England.  From these facts it appears Margaret and her father Edward migrated to Boston sometime before Margaret’s marriage to Edward Preston in 1651.

In 1651, William Towne purchased land in the neighboring town of Topsfield in the Massachusetts Bay Colony from William Paine, which consisted of forty acres divided into plow land, meadow, and unplowed land.  William sold his Salem property to Henry Bullock in 1652 and bought additional land in Topsfield in 1656.

On 26 Mar 1656, Robert Smith and Mary French were married in Rowley Village in the Massachusetts Bay Colony.  Robert had completed his indentured servitude period and was now a successful “tailor” in the Boxford and Topsfield townships and owned his own farm in the area.

On 6 Oct 1656, Mary Cheever was born to Daniel Cheever and Esther (Hester) in the town of Cambridge in the Massachusetts Bay Colony.

In 1656, eleven-year-old William Taylor arrived in the ship "Speedwell" alone with a number of other unaccompanied young boys.  William was probably an apprentice of one of the older passengers on the ship.  Many of the passengers on the ship “Speedwell” settled west of the town of Sudbury in what later became the town of Marlborough in the Massachusetts Bay Colony.

In 1654, Robert Drouin, with his second wife Marie (Chapelier) and their children left the Québec settlement where they were both working for the Jesuits and farming on their own land that had been given to them.  Robert sold the house he had built and provided 600 bricks for the Jesuits to thank them for their help they provided his family when it was in need.  Robert next moved his family back to his original farm he had left in Chateau-Richer and was now renting.  Wife Marie’s opposition to living out in the country appeared to have soften, partly because the family fortunes had greatly improved.  Robert and Marie lived on their farm in Chateau-Richer for the next thirty-years and brought up six more children.  Their improved prosperity was demonstrated in the sized and value of the dowries given to their daughters.  When their first daughter from his first marriage married in 1656 Robert could not give her a dowry at all.  Three years later when daughter Jeanne Drouin was married, Robert was able to give her a milk cow and clothing.  After another three years the first daughter in Robert’s second marriage was given a dowry of mike cow, a calf, clothing and 100 livres in cash.  Eight years later when daughter Marguerite married, she was given a milk cow, clothing, dishes, household utensils and a promise to have a house built for her and her new husband.

On 12 Mar 1656, Ignace Gagne was born to Louis Gagne and Marie (Michel) in the Saint-Anne de-Beaupré settlement in Canada.

In 1656, twenty-six-year-old Marie Anne Le Laboureur, a “filles à marier” woman, sailed from Bayeux, Normandy, France to the Québec settlement in Canada.  Marie had signed a contract to marry Jean Normand when she arrived in Canada.

On 18 Jul 1656, Jean Normand and Marie Anne Le Laboureur, were married in Québec settlement, Canada.  Jean and Marie lived in Charlesbourg; a borough of the Québec settlement located on the northeast perimeter of the settlement that was laid out by Jesuits in a star formation to make it easier to defend against Iroquois warrior attacks.  Jean worked in the community as a “carpenter”.

On 21 Jul 1656, Raphaël  Giroux was born as the third child of Toussaint Giroux and Marie (Godard) in Beauport in Canada.

On 16 Sep 1656, Jean Pilote was born to Léonard Pilote and Denise (Gauthier) in La Rochelle, Normandy, France.

On 3 Dec 1656, Etienne Dumay Jr. was born to Etienne Dumay Sr. and Françoise (Morin) in Sillery in Canada.

In 1656, Marie Bonneau (Lambert) and her seven-year-old son Joseph left France and sailed to Canada following her husband Pierre Bonneau, who had sailed to Canada two years earlier at the encouragement of his father Jean Bonneau.

In 1656, Aimee Caron was born to Robert Caron and Marie (Crevet) in the Québec settlement in Canada.

In 1656, thirty-one-year-old Claude Metru and twenty-eight-year-old Jeanne Crissot were married in Saint-Mern parish Paris, Île-de-France, France.

In 1656, Marie Anne Metru was born to Claude Metru and Jeanne (Crissot) in Paris, Île-de-France, France.  Jeanne (Crissot) died the same year her daughter Marie Anne was born, possibly from the child birth complications.

In 1658, fifty years after the founding of the Québec settlement in Canada by Samuel de Champlain, the population of Canada was just under 3,000 people.  Canada was made up of a handful of “nobles”, forming a small aristocratic administration, a few "bourgeois" merchants and traders engaged in the lucrative fur trade, some missionaries and their aides, and hundreds farmers grouped near the shores of the St-Lawrence River, around the settlements of Québec, Trois-Rivières and Ville Marie.  The population of Canada, although very diversified, had grown very slowly.  The Native peoples living in the St. Lawrence River Valley, felt threatened from both sides (the warring Iroquois and the expanding French settlements) and constantly launched attacks on the expanding French settlements.  The uneasiness in Canada was enhanced further by some bickering that existed among the religious communities and by their political interference in the affairs of the Québec Administrative Council.  All these factors hindered the effective operation of the affairs of the Canada Colony.  The Governor Lauzon was recalled to France in 1656.  His son Charles Lauzon-Charny succeeded him but returned to France two years later.  The King of France, Louis XIV, realized that the only efficient way to protect his interests in the Canada was to increase the number of French settlers there.

In 1656, King Louis XIV of France authorized a system of indentured servants in which a person wishing to immigrate would sign a “contract of engagement”.  They called the contract a "36-mois" (thirty-six months), owing to the three-year period of the contract.  During the contract period the engagé (indentured servant) was denied citizenship, marriage and prohibited from becoming involved in the fur trade.  The purpose of the new contract system was to supply needed laborers to the Canada merchants, allow the newcomers a period of adjustment and an opportunity to learn the skills needed for a lifestyle in Canada they could choose or reject.  If both parties were satisfied, the immigrants could hope to obtain a tract of land where they could settle on in Canada.  The merchants, on the other hand, had to assume the cost of the return trip, food for the three-years of the contract, plus a sum of from 50 to 100 livres depending on the terms of the contract based on the trade and skills of the applicant.

YEAR 1658 (16 paras)
============================================================

In 1639, Solomon Johnson, his wife Eleanor (Croft) and their four-year-old son John Johnson sailed from England and arrived in the Massachusetts Bay Colony.  Solomon was an original proprietor in the town of Sudbury, having received twenty-eight acres.  He was made “freeman” in 1645.  Having bought land in the “Great River Meadow” of West Sudbury, in 1651, he was called "Taylor of Watertown ".  In 1658, Solomon received a grant of one hundred thirty acres in the "two-mile strip" of Sudbury, and later he was given ten more acres when a new road was built running through his property.

In 1651, the village of Littleton in the Massachusetts Bay Colony was one of the original “Praying Indian Villages”.  Rev. John Eliot, known as the “Apostle to the Indians”, began an effort to organize the Massachusetts’ Native American people into Christian Villages.  With the backing of Oliver Cromwell and £12,000 sterling given him by the English government, John Eliot began a long-term mission to translate the Bible into the Algonquin language.  Although he was a Puritan, Eliot was also a humanitarian and he felt that the best way to assure the Native American peoples’ survival in the midst of heavy English land-pressure was to organize the Native Americans into English towns and English lifestyles.  The Native people were to convert to Christianity, receive deeds to their property in new towns, live in English houses, wear English clothes, and worship Puritan style in church meeting houses.  Between 1651 and 1658, John Eliot and his assistant Daniel Gookin organized seven Praying Indian Villages in Massachusetts Bay Colony.  All told there were over 14 such villages established in Massachusetts Bay Colony each with between 45 to 60 Native inhabitants.

In 1658, Oliver Cromwell died and his third son Richard was appointed the second ruling Lord Protector of England, Scotland and Ireland.  Unlike his father, Richard Cromwell lacked military experience and as such failed to gain respect or support from the New Model Army.  In 1659 Richard was ‘persuaded’ to resign from his position as Lord Protector and exiled himself to France.

David Stirling was an exiled  Scottish soldier who had been sent to the New England Colonies to get him out of England and was the father of twenty-two-year-old William Sterling who had immigrated to the New England Colonies and was now living in Haverhill in the Massachusetts Bay Colony.

Note:  In 1658, the English Parliament passed an ‘Act of Uniformity’ defining anyone not belonging to the Anglican Church (Church of England) as being a nonconformist.

The French became interested in the Senegal area, particularly the Senegal River and the large river valley.  In 1626 France set up of a monopoly company the “Norman Company” made up of merchants from Dieppe and Rouen areas of France to exclusive trade with the people of Senegal.  The “Norman Company” was dissolved later in 1658 and replace by new “Company of Cape Vert and Senegal”.  The start and stop of a series of monopole trading companies occurred in Senegal the same way it occurred in Canada during the same period.  Just as happened in Canada, Frenchmen who went to work in the trading monopolies in Senegal were not allowed to take French wives and Frenchwomen were also not allow to travel to the French Colony.  The young adventured single Frenchmen partnered or married native women in Senegal as occurred in Canada.  See a map on the next page showing the areas where the Moors/Berber people lived.

The basilica is an important Catholic sanctuary, which is visited by about a half-million pilgrims each year.  The basilica was initially built as a shrine to honor Saint Ann. YEAR 1660 (15 paras)
============================================================

On 30 Jan 1660, after the fall of the Cromwell’s dictatorships in England, the son of the dead King Charles I was placed on the throne and became King Charles II.  The new King was forced to agree to a restoration agreement with the English Parliament, which gave the king a standing army and allowed him to purge the officials responsible for his father’s execution.  In exchange King Charles II was force agreed to honor the “Petition of Right” and accept limited income.  The “Petition of Right” is a major document in England’s constitutional set of laws that sets out specific liberties of the English subjects (people) that the king is prohibited from infringing upon.  Passed on 7 Jun 1628, the Petition contains restrictions on non-Parliamentary taxation, forced billeting of soldiers, imprisonment without cause, and the use of martial law.

In 1660, following the English Restoration, King Charles II sought to streamline the administration of the English American colonies.  King Charles II and his government began a process that brought a number of the colonies under direct English crown control.  One reason for these actions was to reduce the cost of administration of individual colonies, but another significant reason was the regulation of trade.  Throughout the 1660s, the English Parliament passed a number of laws to regulate the trade of the colonies, collectively called the “Navigation Acts”, which forced all cargo coming and out of England to be carried only on English owned ships.  The American colonists’ owned ships were not considered English ships and their use in shipping goods was prohibited by the new “Navigation Acts”.  The American Colonies had established a significant trading networks with other English colonies and with other European countries and their New World colonies, especially Spain and the Dutch Republic.  The “Navigation Acts” in effect turned the existing American colonial merchants who transported goods in and out of the colonies into outlawed smugglers and significantly increasing the cost of doing business and the cost of imported goods into the American Colonies.

In 1660, the Massachusetts Bay Colony’s General Court ordered the name of the new town being established to be called Marlborough.  The court order read:  "It is ordered. That every person y' claims any interest in the town of Marlborough, shall pay to all public charge, both for the minister and for all other town charges that have arisen about the plantation to this day from the beginning thereof, according to their proportion in a rate now presented with said proportion due ; every person to pay at or before 10 Nov 1660 or else lose all legal interest in the aforesaid plantation; that is to say, four pence an acre for each acre of their house lots to the Minister, and three pence for all the estate that hath been kept or brought to keep, being found in the town or about the town ; and nine pence an acre for every acre of their house lots to town charges, till all the debts that are due from the town to them that have been employed by the town or the plantation thereof."  The Notice was signed by:  Edmund Rice, William Kerly, Thomas King, Henry Kerly, Solomon Johnson, John Howe, Richard Newton, Christopher Banister, William Ward, John Johnson, Thomas Goodnow and John Ruddocke.

On 27 Oct 1660, Roger Preston was allowed to keep and sell strong liquors for the entertainment of strangers as an “inn keeper”, in the town of Salem in the Massachusetts Bay Colony.

In 1660, William Ward moved his family inland nine miles west from the town of Sudbury to the new town of Marlborough in the Massachusetts Bay Colony.  William Ward was chosen the first deacon for the new Marlborough town church.  William was given fifty acres of land for his home and farming in the new community.

From 1644 to 1647, François Bêlanger and Masse Gravel worked a concession (land given to them by the Governor) together farming on the Beaupré coast.  In 1647, Masse Gravel left the farm.  Later on, 24 May 1655, the two settled up and François bought Masse's half of the farm and land for the sum of one hundred livres per arpent, to be paid in 10 installments of 116 livres 6 sols 8 deniers (for a total of 350 livres), each due 24 June, Saint-Jean-Baptiste Day.  François secured this debt by pledging all of his property as well as the present and future inheritance of his children.  On 23 Mar 1660, Masse Gravel declared that his former partner had paid up in full and owed him nothing more.  In this same year, both of them became “church wardens” in the parish of Chateau-Richer.  The first notarized act found which includes François was drawn up on 8 Sep 1647 by Claude Lecoustre.  François was required to pay Pierre Legardeur of Repentigny the sum of one hundred livres for the purchase of some wheat.  To guarantee the loan, he again put up all of his property as security.  Based on the major land transactions François was involved in it is clear he had given up the mason trade and was spending all his time farming.

In 1660, after the end of the “English Civil War” and the restoration of the English monarchy with King Charles II, the English began to wonder why they had tolerated the presence of the Dutch Colony on a territory wedged between their New England and Virginia Colonies.

On 11 Jan 1660, Nicolas Bélanger and Marie de Rainville were married in Beauport, Canada.  Nicolas worked as a “salt merchant” in Beauport and farmed thirty-four acres of land in Beauport on the St. Lawrence River shoreline. In the 1660s, the Iroquois continued to control the forest areas of Canada, raiding to the edges of the walled settlements of Québec, Trois-Rivières and Villa Marie. On 5 Feb 1663, a series of big earthquakes occurred in the St. Lawrence River Valley. Note:  At the same time diseases were decimating the Native American people in the English American colonies, a breakout of ”Bubonic Plague” occurred in 1665 in England.  The disease quickly spread from parish to parish across England killing thousands of people.  Bubonic Plague was known as the “Black Death” and had been known in Europe for centuries.  It was a ghastly disease. The victim’s skin turned black in patches and inflamed glands or ‘buboes’ in the groin, combined with compulsive vomiting, swollen tongue and splitting headaches made it a horrible, agonizing killer.

The 1665 version of the “Black Death” plague began in London in the poor, overcrowded parish of St. Giles-in-the-Field.  It started slowly at first and by May of 1665, 43 had died.  In June 6,137 more people died, in July 17,036 people died and at its peak in August, 31,159 people died.  In all, 15% of the English population perished during that terrible 1665 summer.  Incubation period took a mere four to six days and when the plague appeared in a household, the house was sealed, thus condemning the whole family to death!  These houses were distinguished by a painted red cross on the door and the words, “Lord have mercy on us”.  At night the dead corpses were brought out in answer to the cry,” Bring out your dead” and put in carts and taken away to the plague earth dugout pits.  The plague spread to most areas in England.  The city of York was badly affected.  King Charles II and his Court staff fled London and moved out to Oxford to avoid the plague.

In 1643, the Iroquois Confederation began attacking, using guerrilla raids (quick surprise attack against a larger force and then a quick escape), on the new Ville Marie and the Trois-Rivières settlements.  The Iroquois had recently received a large number of Dutch firearms, they called “fire sticks” making them a much more formable adversary.  The Canada Colony had about 350 inhabitants at the time and the “Iroquois Five Nation Confederation” had more than 12,000 members, including 2,200 highly disciplined warriors.  The Iroquois seized control of the fur trade and blocked French access to the interior of North America.  The Iroquois also prevented other native tribes from trading with the French.  From 1645 to 1665 the Canada colony and its settlements were under constant attacks.  The Canada settlers could not fish, hunt, clear land or plough their fields without fear of being ambushed.  About 10% of the Canada population and most of its livestock were killed during this twenty-year period.

In 1665, the pleas of the colonists of Canada for assistance in their struggle with the Native American Iroquois Confederation of tribes were answered with the arrival of the first large number of French regular troops in Canada, the Carignan-Salières Regiment.  The Regiment had been delayed in La Rochelle France, to allow Catholic Jesuits to convert the Protestant Huguenots soldiers who were members of the regiment to Catholicism.  An additional delay occurred to allow time to construct canoes the regiment would need to travel up the St Lawrence River.

Between Jun and Sep 1665, some of 1,200 soldiers and their officers arrived in Québec, under the leadership of Lt. General Alexander de Prouville, Sieur de Tracy.  The Regiment established a series of forts along the Richelieu River and conducted a successful campaign into the land of the Mokawk-Iroquois.  The defeat of the Mohawk-Iroquois, led to a long period of peace for the French colony, which permitted it to prosper.  King Louis XIV's plan also allowed the Carignan-Salières Regiment soldiers at the completion of the conflict to stay and become settlers in Canada.  404 of the Regiment’s soldiers accepted offers of land in Canada and remained in the colony, many of whom married the newly arrived “filles du Roi” girls.  Seven (7) of the Carignan-Salières Regiment’s soldiers are directly connected to the Mary Ann Goodwater Branch of the Winifred Eloise Coss’ family tree.

In 1665, Jean Talon arrived with the Carignan-Salières Regiment as Quebec Colony’s first administrative officer over the settlement’s agriculture, education, justice, and trade.

Chambly was the home to the massive Fort Chambly, built with local stone between 1709 and 1711 in the style of classic French fortifications.  The fort was built on land south of the St. Lawrence River at the mouth of a large basin, on the site of successive wooden forts dating back to 1665.  Fort Chambly was the largest in a series of fortifications on the shores of what was known as the Iroquois River (later known as the Chambly River, finally becoming the Richelieu River).  The site was originally called Fort Saint-Louis, but soon came to be known by the name of its first commanding officer, Jacques de Chambly, to whom the surrounding seigniory was granted in 1672.  It was intended to protect Canada in general (and Ville Marie in specific) from attacks of hostile native warriors and English soldiers. On 2 May 1670, the “Hudson’s Bay Company” (HBC) was chartered making it the oldest incorporated joint-stock merchandising company in the English-speaking world.  HBC was a fur trading business that was entwined with the colonization of North America, particularly in Canada.  In the 1600s, fur trading emerged as a major commercial enterprise in North America due to European demand for fur felt hats made from beaver pelts.  Two French traders were the first to propose the idea of forming a trading company to reach the interior of the North America via Hudson Bay.  After failing to obtain French support on their idea the French traders went to England in 1665 and interested Prince Rupert, the cousin of England’s King Charles II in their idea.  Prince Rupert persuaded his cousin the king and several English merchants to back the venture.  The “Hudson’s Bay Company” was granted wide powers, including exclusive trading rights in the territory crossed by rivers flowing into Hudson Bay.  This vast region was named “Rupert’s Land” after Prince Rupert.

On 9 Jan 1666, the soldiers of the French “Carignan-Salières Regiment” invaded the Iroquois homeland south of Lake Ontario.  The French invasion force of 600 solders was led by the new Governor General of Canada, Daniel de Remy de Courcelle.  A party of Algonquin Native warriors were expected to guide the French army but they failed to appear.  The French soldiers, carrying their arms and supplies on their backs were unaccustomed to marching in snow-shoes in deep powdery snow and quickly became exhausted.  The intense cold weather made their ordeal even worse.  Without native guides the French army got lost and frequently had to retrace their path.  By 15 Feb 1666, the 600 French soldiers were completely lost and luckily stumbled upon the Schenectady settlement Province of New York, which was 20-miles northwest of the town of Albany New York that was recently taken from the Dutch by the British, two-years earlier.

Later in July 1666, being aware that the French army was preparing a new attack on Mohawk lands, a group of Mohawk warriors ambushed some French officers in Lake Champlain area, killing seven and taking four prisoner.  In response, Canada’s Governor de Tracy had several members of an Oneida Iroquois delegation, who were in the Quebec City at the time, held as captives.  Chief Canaqueese and three other Mohawk leaders traveled to the Quebec  City to exchange prisoners with the French and ratify a peace treaty between the Iroquois and the French.  These Mohawk leaders were troubled by the lack of support that their English and Dutch allies were providing, and had decided to seek peace with the French.  The delegation arrived on 28 Aug 1666, but the talks soon broke down and Governor de Tracy imprisoned the four Mohawk leaders.

By the fall of 1666, violence between the French and Mohawk resumed.  A second French invasion force led by Viceroy Alexandre de Prouville, with of about 1,300 men of the “Carignan-Salières Regiment” moved out looking for Iroquois.  They found deserted Mohawk villages and destroy them and the crops stored and growing in the area.  Viceroy Prouville seized all the Mohawk lands in the name of the king of France.  Chief Canaqueese was released from custody by Intendant Jean Talon, and sent back to Mohawk country with an official offer of peace.  The peace offer required the Mohawk’s to release some of their Huron, Algonquin, and French captives, who they had been adopted into Mohawk tribes’ villages and the Iroquois native society.  After Chief Canaqueese made a series of diplomatic missions in spring of 1667 to negotiate with French, on provisions of a peace treaty between the Mohawks and French an agreement was finally achieved and the Mohawk released some of their captives.  The peace settlement lasted for more than a decade.  Some of the Mohawk people later migrated to Ville Marie settlement area and settling nearby in the southern part of the St. Lawrence Valley, including Chief Canaqueese himself in 1667.

In 1670, England’s King Charles II signed a treaty with French King Louis XIV in which he agreed to convert to Catholicism and support France’s war against the Protestant Dutch in return for money.  King Charles II’s wife Queen Catherine had failed to produce a child and the king arranged for his younger brother’s oldest daughter Mary, a Protestant, to married her first cousin, the Protestant Prince William III of the Dutch Republic known as “William of Orange”.  The arranged royal marriage will have a major impact on the future of England. In 1675, Native-Americans in New England made their last major effort to drive the English colonists out of their ancestorial territory in the “King Philip’s War”.  The war lasted a little more than a year.  In the war 12 of the colonial towns were destroyed and many more were damaged and the New England colonies’ economy were ruined.  The New Englanders lost one-tenth of all men available for military service in the war.  Over half of New England's towns were attacked.  The war was named for King Philip, the Wampanoag Chief who adopted the English name Philip due to the friendly relations between his father and the Mayflower settlers.  Chief Philip led a coalition of the Nipmuck, Pocumtuc and Narraganset tribes in an uprising against the English colonists and their allies, the Mohegans and the Mohawks tribes.  The war was disastrous for the native-Americans resulting in great losses of lives and the loss of much of their native lands.

None were encountered.  On 2 Nov 1675, “King Philip’s War” was formally declared against the Narragansetts Native American tribe.  On 19 Dec 1675, Josiah Winslow, Governor of the Plymouth Colony was appointed commander-in-chief of the Army of the United Colonies.  Lt. Upham was assigned to the 4th Company of the Massachusetts Regiment commanded by Captain Isaac Johnson.  The United Colonies’ Army had three Regiments, a Massachusetts, a Plymouth and a Connecticut regiment.

The major and decisive battle of the King Philip’s War occurred in the “Battle at the Great Swamp Fort”.  On 19 Dec 1675, hoping to prevent a spring Indian onslaught, Plymouth Colony’s Governor Josiah Winslow gathered the Army of the United Colonies and attacked a massive Narragansett and Wampanoag village fortification near the Great Swamp in West Kingston in the Rhode Island Colony.  The following account of the battle appeared in the New England Historical and Genealogical Register Jan 1886.

“About one o’clock in the afternoon on the 19 Dec 1675 the Army of the United Colonies came upon an Indian fortress built on a small island in the midst of a large cedar swamp.  Access to the fort was restricted to a strongly defended single-file path through the swamp land.  The severer cold of the previous day and night had frozen the muddy surface of swamp land near the fortified island allowing the colonial soldiers to use temporary alternate paths to approach the fortified camp.  The fort was surrounded by water and a clay wall covered with felled trees.  The barrier was impressive, but was not finished.  An unfinished opening in the wall provided a weakness in the defense the Colonial Army took advantage of in their attack.  If the wall had been finished and the ground was not frozen an attack on the fort would have been suicide.”

In 1675, New England had about 80,000 colonists.  They lived in 110 towns, of which 64 were in the Massachusetts Bay Colony, which then included the southwestern portion of Maine.  New Englanders had about 16,000 men of military age who were almost all a part of the local town militias, as universal training was require in all New England towns.  Many towns had built strong garrison houses for defense, and others also had stockades wall enclosing most of the town’s houses.  All of the towns were strengthened as the “King Philip’s War” progressed.  Some poorly populated towns were abandoned, when they did not have enough men to defend them.  Each town had a local militia based on all eligible men in the town, who all had to supply their own arms.  Only those who were too old, too young, or were disabled or in the clergy were excused from military service.  The town militias were usually only minimally trained and initially did relatively poorly against the Native warriors, until more effective training and tactics were devised as the war progressed.  Joint forces of colonial militia volunteers and Native American allies fighting together proved to be the most effective way to fight the opposing warring Native warriors.
