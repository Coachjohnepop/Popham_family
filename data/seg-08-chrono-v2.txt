YEAR 1754 (4 paras)
============================================================
In 1754, the “French and Indian War” broke out as the fourth and final war in North America between the British colonies and their Native American allies against the French Canada colony with their own Native American allies.  At the start of the war, the France had only about 60,000 French settlers in Canada, compared to about 2 million people in the British colonies.  The British colonists were supported at various times by the Native American Iroquois, Catawba, and Cherokee tribes.  The France on the other hand were supported by the larger Native American Wabanaki Confederacy, which included members of the Abenaki, Mi'kmaq, Algonquin, Lenape, Ojibwa, Ottawa, Shawnee, and Wyandot Native tribes.  The outnumbered French soldiers and Canadian colonists were particularly dependent on their Native American allies.  The war started over a dispute for control of the strategic land at the confluence of the Allegheny River and Monongahela River called the “Forks of the Ohio”.  The French had constructed Fort Duquesne on the site, which today the city of Pittsburgh in Pennsylvania.  The fighting started on 28 May 1754, when a British military unit led by twenty-two-year-old Lt. Colonel George Washington, ambushed a small French force at the “Battle of Jumonville Glen” (present day Hopwood, Pennsylvania 50 miles south of Pittsburgh ).  The conflict quickly exploded across the British and French colonies in North America and extended to the seizure of hundreds of French merchant ships at sea by the British Royal Navy.  The British Navy next implemented an effective blockage of the French Canada colony preventing the France from sending additional French soldiers to fight against the British Army supporting the British Colonies.  With numbers on their side the British forces overwhelmed the French forcing them to surrender to the British Army after the fall of Quebec City in 1759.
The conflict subsided a bit when the Iroquois lost their Dutch allies in the New Amsterdam Colony after the English took over the Dutch Colony in 1664.  The French then attempted to gain the Iroquois as an ally against the English, but the Iroquois refused to break their alliance with the English and frequently fought against the French.  The English-Iroquois alliance would reach its zenith during the later 1754 “French and Indian War”, which saw the French being expelled from North America.
In 1754, North America was divided into British, France and Spanish colonies.  See a map on the next page showing the major French and British fighting actions in the “French and Indian War”:
On 28 May 1754, a war started when a British Colonial Militia unit led by a 22-year-old Lt. Colonel George Washington ambushed a small French force in the “Battle of Jumonville Glen”.  The French reacted to the attack on their soldiers and the “French and Indian War” started.  The war in North America was between the France’s Canadian Provinces and their native Indian allies against Great Britain and its North American colonies.  Both the French and English sent large armies from Europe to fight in North America.  One of the first results of the war occurred when the French Acadians living in Nova Scotia refused to swear allegiance to the British king and were expelled from their homes.  The French Acadians living in Nova Scotia were forced to scatter throughout the North American.  Some of the Acadian (pronounced ‘a-cay-DJYEN in French) settled in the French Louisiana colony.  The word “Cajun” derives from the word “Acadian”.

YEAR 1756 (2 paras)
============================================================
In 1756, two years after the British and French starting fighting in North America the “Seven Years’ War” started Europe splitting the area into two coalitions: on one side was Great Britain, Prussia, Portugal and some small German states.  On the other side were France, Holy Roman Empire, Russia, Spain and Sweden.  Not much was accomplished in the conflict in Europe other than the rise of Prussia as a major European military power.  The war ended with the “Treaty of Paris” signed in 1763.  The treaty formally ended the “Seven Years' War” in Europe and the  “French and Indian War” in the North American, and marked the beginning of an era of British dominance outside of Europe.  Great Britain gained all the France's possessions in North America in the “Treaty of Paris”.  Spain also lost Florida to the British, but gained the French territories west of the Mississippi River.
Both the French and the British sent more soldiers to North America.  France sent a new commander, Louis-Joseph Montcalm to led the French forces.  In May 1756, Great Britain and France declared war on each other.  The French had the upper hand in the fighting in 1756 and 1757 in North America.  The British soon sent more military forces and the British Royal Navy took control of the Atlantic shipping lanes, preventing France from sending more military help.

YEAR 1763 (9 paras)
============================================================
In 1763, George Grenville became the new British Prime Minister.  Great Britain had just won the “Seven Year War” and the “French and Indian War” against the French.  The victories were great, but Great Britain was in a desperate need of money to pay off its large war debts and the ongoing in the cost of the maintaining 10,000 British troops in North America.  The primary reason for retaining such a large British military force in North America after the war was to prevent the demobilizing the army and putting some 1,500 loyal British military officers out of work.  It became politically prudent to retain a large peacetime British army, but citizens back in Great Britain were averse to maintaining a standing army at home, so it was necessary to leave and garrison of troops in North America.  Raising taxes in Britain was also out of the question, since there had been virulent (strong) protests when a tax on the production of cider had been proposed.  Another source of money was need to pay the British army garrison in North America.
On 7 Oct 1763, King George III issued a “Royal Proclamation” that forbade all settlements west of a line drawn along the center of the Appalachian Mountains, which was delineated as the eastern boundary of a Native American Reserve.  Exclusion from the vast region of land in the Ohio River Valley created great discontent between the British government and wealthy colonial land speculators and new settlers looking for land to settle on and farm.  The proclamation and its probation of access to western lands was one of the first significant areas of dispute between Britain and its American Colonies and will become a contributing factor leading to the later American Revolution.  See the map below that shows the “Proclamation Line of 1763”.
Back in 1763, after the “French and Indian War” and the signing of the “Treaty of Paris” the French lost all their North American possessions.  The end of the war did not however solve the issues over Vermont.  In 1775, after the outbreak of the American Revolutionary War, the “Green Mountain Boys” assisted a force from Connecticut, led by Benedict Arnold, that capturing the British fort at Ticonderoga.  Thereafter, the Continental Congress in Philadelphia directed the New York colony's revolutionary congress to fund and equip Allen's militia as a ranger regiment of the Continental Army, which it did.
In 1800, in the secret treaty of “Third Treaty of San Ildefonso”, France regained the western area of North America from Spain, which France had lost after the “French and Indian war” back in the 1763 “Treaty of Paris”.
In 1763, after the “Treaty of Paris” Great Britain took over Canada under the rule of Royal Governor James Murray who was benign, guaranteed the French Canadians they would be allowed to retain their traditional rights and customs.  Great Britain’s King George III issued a “Royal Proclamation” united the three Québec districts into the “Province of Québec”.  It was the British who were the first to use the name "Québec" to refer to a territory beyond Québec City.  The British tolerated the Catholic Church and protected the traditional social and economic structure of the new Province of Québec.  The people of Québec Province responded with one of the highest birth rates ever recorded, 65 births per thousand per year.  Most of the existing French laws were retained within a new system of British courts, all under the command of the British governor.  The goal was to satisfy the “Francophiles” (French settlers), albeit to the annoyance of British merchants back in Great Britain.
Until 1763, the Hudson Bay Company (HBC) struggled with the French for control of the fur trade in southern Rupert’s Land.  In the early years, a series of naval and land battles took place on the Hudson and James Bays.  In 1713, as part of the “Treaty of Utrecht”, France acknowledged England’s claim to the Hudson Bay area.  For the next 60 years, the HBC erected posts at the mouths of all major rivers flowing into the Hudson and James Bays.  HBC’s reluctance to establish trading posts in the interior of Rupert’s Land allowed competitors to outflank the HBC by travelling into the interior to trade with Native peoples rather than waiting for them to come to the coast.  The Native trappers used this intense rivalry to extract greater returns for their furs.
After the 1763 “Treaty of Paris”, that force France out of North America, the HBC’s French rivals were replaced by a much more formidable opposition, the Montréal-based “Overland tide Network” taken over by the British.  By 1774, HBC trade had been undercut enough that the governor and the committee embarked on an aggressive policy of inland expansion beginning with the building of Cumberland House on the lower Saskatchewan River and the development of the Red River Colony in Manitoba, Canada.  In 1821, a merger of the two companies was arranged, and the British Parliament confirmed and extended the HBC’s monopoly privileges in North America out to the Pacific Coast.

YEAR 1774 (5 paras)
============================================================
Back in 1686 the land known as “Towtaid”, which was 50-miles west of Boston, had been purchased from the Nipmuc Native people by a Joshua Lamb and other businessmen from the town of Roxbury in the Province of Massachusetts Bay.  The area was renamed “Strawberry Hill” after its profuse wild strawberries.  Later the Massachusetts’s General Court renamed the area Leicester, after the shire in England that Samuel Green’s family came from.  Leicester's standing militia company at the beginning of the American Revolutionary War marched to Lexington and Concord to aid in the defeat of the British regulars.  The term "Minuteman" originated in Leicester, when Colonel William Henshaw declared at a 1774 meeting of the “Committee on Safety” said: "we must have companies of men ready to march upon a minute's notice."
On 20 May 1774, the British Parliament passed the “Massachusetts Government Act” that revoked the 1691 charter of the Province of Massachusetts Bay and appointed a new British Royal governor over the former province with wide-ranging powers.  The Massachusetts colonial leaders argued the act altered the basic historical structure of American colonial government and vehemently opposed the new act and would not let it operate.
In Oct 1774, the “Province of Massachusetts Bay” came to an end, when members of the General Court of Massachusetts met in violation of the “Massachusetts Government Act” and established the “Massachusetts Provincial Congress”.  Although the new British Governor Gage continued military rule in Boston, the new provincial congress had in effect rule over the rest of the former province.  Hostilities between the British troops and rebel militia colonists six months later started the American Revolutionary in April 1775 at the towns of Lexington and Concord, and continued with the siege of the City of Boston.
On 29 Nov 1774, Nathaniel Powers was born to Josiah Powers Jr. and Mary (Robbins) in the town of Chesterfield in the New Hampshire Colony.
In 1774, the “Québec Act of 1774” was enacted by Great Britain’s Parliament setting up procedures for the governance in the “Province of Québec”.  The Act officially recognized: French civil laws in British Québec Province, Roman Catholicism as the official religion in Québec and French as the official language in Québec.  Among the other components of the “Québec Act” were the restoration of the use of the French civil law for private matters, while maintaining the use of the English common law for public administration (including criminal prosecution), rewording  of the oath of allegiance in the Québec Province so that it no longer made reference to the Protestant faith, and guaranteed free practice of the Catholic faith.  The Act's principal purpose was to prevent French Québec from joining the 13 American Colonies in their rebellion against British rule.  The fact that the “Québec Act” granted the French colonists some independence and control over civil matters helped incited resistance to British rule in the American colonies, which observe the obvious differences between the way the French Canadians and the loyal British Americans colonists were being treated.

YEAR 1775 (6 paras)
============================================================
A group of men, including Edmund Rice, who were living in the town of Watertown had recently been granted the right to establish a new plantation called “New Concord” in the Massachusetts Bay Colony.  Edmund Rice with other men established the bounders for the new settlement.  The first house lots comprised a few acres each.  The homes were designed to be built close together for security and safety.  Before Edmund started to build his new home, he sold his property in New Concord and bought a house and six acres from widow Axdell.  Edmund next leased for six years a farm of Henry Dunster, the first president of Harvard College.  The lease required Edmund to pay Henry Dunster thirty bushels of corn a year for two years, then fifty bushels each for the next two years and finally one hundred bushels for the last two years in equal proportions of wheat, Indian corn and rye.  At the end of the six years Edmund bought the farm from Henry.  Later Edmund bought nine more acres and a house that expanded his homestead.  The homestead stayed in the Rice family for many generations.  The town of Concord, later in 1775, would be the sight of the first battle of the American Revolution.
In Apr 1775, Nathaniel Farnham enlisted in Captain Jonathan Wentworth’s company of Colonel Enoch Poor’s 2nd New Hampshire regiment, which in Aug 1775 was designated the “11th Regiment of Foot” in the Continental Army for eight months.  A month later he participated in the “Capture of Fort Ticonderoga” where Ethan Allen and his “Green Mountain Boys” under the command of Colonel Benedict Arnold defeated the British soldiers at the fort and captured the fort’s British cannons.  The cannons and other armaments were later transported back to the City of Boston by Colonel Henry Knox and used to fortify Dorchester Heights and break the British siege of Boston.
In June 1775, Josiah Powers Jr. enlisted in Captain Jacob Hinds Company, of Colonel James Reed’s 3rd New Hampshire Regiment, which in Aug 1775 was designated the “3rd Regiment of Foot” in the Continental Army and fought against the British forces during the American Revolutionary War.  The regiment saw action on 17 June 1775, at the “Battle of Bunker Hill”, which is more properly known as the “Battle of Breeds Hill”, in the Siege of Boston by the British.
On 28 Oct 1775, a census was conducted of the people living in the town of Temple, New Hampshire.  Zebadiah Johnson was listed as: 5 males under age 16, 1 male age 16-50, 0 males over 60 years old, 1 Person in Army, 4 females, 0 guns, 0 powder.
In 1775, Zebadiah Johnson was selected as one of two men to serve as the town of Temple’s; Municipal, Political and Religious Officers.  Zebadiah also served a one of the town’s two “Tithingman” (English Peace Officer).
In 1775, the “American Revolutionary War” broke out and British Canada became a target for American forces, that sought to liberate the French population from British rule.  In Sep 1775 the American Continental Army began a two-pronged invasion of Canada, with one army’s goal of capturing the Montréal City while another army traveled through the wilderness of what is now the State of Maine toward the goal of capturing Québec City.  The two armies joined forces, but were defeated in the “Battle of Québec”, in which the American Continental General Richard Montgomery was killed.  The Continental Army was driven back out of Canada and back into New York by the arrival of a large army of British soldiers and mercenary hired German soldiers in June 1776.

YEAR 1776 (7 paras)
============================================================
Between 1718 and 1776, over 52,000 convicts and unemployed youth were transported to the American Colonies, primarily to Virginia and Maryland.  The outbreak of the American Revolution ended the process in 1776 in America.  From 1788 to 1869, more than 160,000 prisoners and unemployed youth were sent instead to the British Colony in Australia.
On 17 Mar 1776, the British were forced to evacuate the City of Boston, ending the siege of the city and bringing the city under colonial rebel control.  On May 1, 1776, the provincial congress adopted a resolution declaring the Province of Massachusetts to be independent of the crown; this was followed up on 4 Jul 1776 by the “Declaration of Independence”, declaring the independence of all thirteen of the American Colonies.  In Oct 1779, a new Constitution of the Commonwealth of Massachusetts was agreed upon and went into effect in Jun 1780.  In elections held in in Oct 1780, John Hancock was elected the first Governor of the State of Massachusetts along with representatives to the commonwealth's first General Court.
In Apr 1776, Nathaniel Farnham was involved in the “Battle of Cedars” (Cedars was a community north of the Saint Lawrence River 28 miles west of City of Montreal in British Canada).  Brigadier General Benedict Arnold, commanding the American Continental military garrison camped at Montreal, and in Apr 1776 placed a detachment of his troops in the community of Cedars, after hearing rumors that the British and their Native American allies were making military preparations to the west of Montreal.  The Continental garrison surrendered on 19 May 1776 after a confrontation with a combined force of British and Native American warriors led by Captain George Forster.  American reinforcements that were sent to Cedars were also captured after a brief skirmish the next day.  All of the Continental Army captives were eventually released after negotiations between British Captain Forster and General Arnold, who had arrived with a sizable Continental force into the area.  The terms of the agreement required the American Continental Army to release an equal number of British prisoners, but the deal was renounced by the Continental Congress, and no British prisoners were freed.  Colonel Timothy Bedel and Lieutenant Issacs Butterfield, leaders of the American Continental force at the Cedars, were both court martialed (a military trial) and cashiered (a ritual dismissal from the Continental Army) for their roles in the Cedars garrison surrender.
On 4 Jul 1776, Zebadiah Johnson was one of 84 men living in the town of Temple in New Hampshire who pledged their support for a Continental Congress’ resolution professing one’s wiliness to risk one’s life and fortune, and take up arms in resistance to the hostile attacks of the British Fleets and Armies against the United American Colonies.
On 18 Jul 1776, Zebadiah Johnson was serving as a Sergeant in the Colonel Isaac Wyman’s New Hampshire Militia that had mustered two days earlier at Keene, New Hampshire and marched to Fort at Number 4 (A stockade fortification protecting Plantation Number 4 located along the Connecticut River in New Hampshire) and then on to Fort Ticonderoga where the regiment stayed until Dec 1776, when the regiment returned to New Hampshire for winter quarters.
In 1776, Josiah Powers Jr. moved his family to the town of Brandon, which is 16 miles north of Rutland Vermont.  Josiah Powers Jr. was a member of the convention with his father that formed the State Constitution of Vermont and later was a member of the 1778 Vermont State Legislature.
On 15 Feb 1776, Benjamin Franklin, Samuel Chase and Charles Carroll travel to the Québec City and offer to make the French speaking area the 14 American colony in the effort to separate the French people in Canada from Britain.  The French Canadians seriously considered the proposal, until the American forces suffered a defeat in their effort to capture Québec City.

YEAR 1782 (1 paras)
============================================================
On 3 Sep 1783, the “Treaty of Paris” was signed ending the American Revolutionary War.  With the war over, the American colonists who had remained loyal to Great Britain were now on the losing side of the war.  Many of the loyal British colonists were expelled or voluntarily moved up to the British provinces in Canada.  Nova Scotia swelled with new residents and the province was partitioned creating a new separate New Brunswick Province.  Many other British loyalists moved out to the area north of the Great Lakes in western portion of the Province of Québec area to settle.  The city of York (present-day Toronto) became the center of activity and later in 1791 became the capitol of Upper Québec.  Between 1782 and 1784, over 5,000 loyal British colonists from the new “Confederation of the United States” entered what is now Ontario Province Canada.  Great Britain granted the new newly arriving British supporting American colonists, 200 acres of land and other items with which they could use to rebuild their new lives.  Great Britain also set up native people’s reserve lands area north of Lakes Superior and Lake Huron for the Native Mohawks Iroquois allies who had fought on the British side in the war and lost their land in the State of New York.

War of 1812
On 18 June 1812, the new United States of America declared war against United Kingdom beginning the “War of 1812”.  Although often treated as a minor footnote to the bloody European war between France and Britain, the “War of 1812” was crucial for the future of United States.  The victory in the war effectively destroyed the Native Americans' ability to resist the United States’ expansion into the large Ohio River valley.  General Andrew Jackson crushed the Native Creek tribe in the “Battle of Horseshoe Bend” in Alabama, while General William Henry Harrison defeated the Native Tecumseh’s Confederation who were allies of the British army at the “Battle of the Thames” in Ontario, Upper Canada.  Abandoned by their British allies, the Native Americans tribes reluctantly surrendered most of their lands north of the Ohio River and in western Alabama to the United States’ government.
Note:  See the Story of Morris Rankin Batie’s Family, and the Story of Carolee, Afton McCance’s Family for more details on the “War of 1812”.
On 8 Jan 1815, the “Battle of New Orleans” was fought in the “War of 1812” between the British Army under Major General Sir Edward Pakenham and the United States Army under Major General Andrew Jackson.  The battle occurred 5 miles southeast of the French Quarter of the city of New Orleans.  The battle was the climax of the five-month “Gulf Campaign” by the British military in their efforts to take over possession of New Orleans, West Florida and the Louisiana Territory.  The battle took place 18 days after the signing of the “Treaty of Ghent”, which formally ended the “War of 1812” between Great Britain and the United States.  The news of the new treaty agreement had not yet reached the United States from Europe.  Despite a large British advantage in the numbers of trained experienced soldiers, the American volunteer forces defeated a poorly executed assault by the British Army in slightly more than 30 minutes.  The Americans suffered roughly 60 casualties, while the British suffered roughly 2,000 casualties, including the deaths of their commander, Major General Sir Edward Pakenham.
The victory in the “War of 1812” allowed the United States to rewrite its boundaries with Spain and solidify control over the lower Mississippi River and the Gulf of Mexico.  Although the United States did not defeat the British Empire, it had fought the world's strongest power to a draw.  Spain recognized the significance of this fact and in 1819 Spanish leaders abandoned Florida and agreed to a United States boundary running across the North American continent to the Pacific Ocean.
Back in 1815, the “Treaty of Ghent” ended the “War of 1812” and reaffirming the United States jurisdiction over the “Michigan Territory”, which included the future State of Wisconsin.  The establishment of American settlements in the Wisconsin Territory was delayed by two Native American tribes fighting in 1827 the “Winnebago War” and later in 1832 the “Black Hawk War” in hopeless efforts to try and stop the settlement of their ancestral homelands.  The defeat of the Native American tribes living in Wisconsin open a large area for new settlements.  On 3 Jul 1836, the “Wisconsin Territory” was created by an act of the U. S. Congress.  The first settlers were drawn to the new area to prospect and mine “lead ore” in southwest of the territory.  By the 1840’s southwest Wisconsin was producing more than half of the lead in the country.  See a map below showing the 1836 Wisconsin and Michigan Territories:
