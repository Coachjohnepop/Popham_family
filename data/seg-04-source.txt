=== p-00451 (1630) ===
Praying Indian Villages

=== p-00452 (1631) ===
In 1651, the village of Littleton in the Massachusetts Bay Colony was one of the original “Praying Indian Villages”.  Rev. John Eliot, known as the “Apostle to the Indians”, began an effort to organize the Massachusetts’ Native American people into Christian Villages.  With the backing of Oliver Cromwell and £12,000 sterling given him by the English government, John Eliot began a long-term mission to translate the Bible into the Algonquin language.  Although he was a Puritan, Eliot was also a humanitarian and he felt that the best way to assure the Native American peoples’ survival in the midst of heavy English land-pressure was to organize the Native Americans into English towns and English lifestyles.  The Native people were to convert to Christianity, receive deeds to their property in new towns, live in English houses, wear English clothes, and worship Puritan style in church meeting houses.  Between 1651 and 1658, John Eliot and his assistant Daniel Gookin organized seven Praying Indian Villages in Massachusetts Bay Colony.  All told there were over 14 such villages established in Massachusetts Bay Colony each with between 45 to 60 Native inhabitants.

=== p-00453 (1631) ===
Rev. John Eliot allowed the Native people to choose the sites for their new villages.  The local Concord Native people led by an early Christian convert, Chief Sachem Tahattawan, requested the "Nashoba" lands close by for their new praying village.  The new Native Plantation of Nashoba was formally granted by Massachusetts Bay Colony General Court in 1651 and was laid out in rectangle 3 x 4 miles.  The area encompassed most of current day Littleton and a portion of Boxborough in Massachusetts.  The Nashoba Praying Village thrived until “King Phillip’s War” in 1675-76, when the Nashoba Native people were rounded up and dumped on Deer Island in the middle of Boston Harbor to freeze and starve.  More on the “King Phillip’s War” will be covered later.  Only a handful of the Native Nashoba people survived the ordeal and were able to return to Nashoba Village.  The Nashoba’s Plantation land was sold to English settlers and by 1714 it was completely in English settlers’ hands and re-incorporated as the Town of Littleton in 1715.  The surviving Native Nashoba people were given a new Native town on a 500-acre tract of rocky hills between Nagog Pond and Fort Pond (which was named after the Native fort located in the area).

=== p-00464 (1632) ===
In May 1655, the Massachusetts Bay Colony’s General Court approved the creation of a new community called Chelmsford near the towns of Concord and Woburn in the colony.  The settlement of Chelmsford was a horrible example of taking land from the Native American people.  The Pennacook Native people (meaning bottom of the hill) had lived in the area selected for the new Chelmsford community for thousands of years.  The Native Americans were Algonquian-speaking people who operated as a network of politically and culturally allied communities rather than a single large tribe.  The major Pennacook villages were built along the rivers in the area.  Life revolved around the seasons with Native women beginning in the spring collecting maple sap and making maple sugar.  Men would burn the forests to add nutrients in the soils for later cultivation.  In late spring the rivers and creeks would swell as the salmon and shad fish making their way up the rivers to spawn.  Many Pennacook villages were built above natural waterfalls that gathered the spawning fish and made it easier to catch them in the late spring.  The delicious “fiddlehead fern” season occurred next followed by other seasons known today, like blueberry and the raspberry seasons.  During the summers, the Native families would disperse out to summer villages and hunting camps.  Women did most of the work of building and maintaining homes as well as farming.  Their main crops were varieties of corn and squash, which they planted along rivers and in meadows.  While they found it difficult to clear the massive old-growth forest trees, the Pennacook’s were experts at manipulating beavers to move their dams and build ponds up and down creeks and brooks, thereby clearing and opening up land for farms that would be essential to the first Europeans settlers who arrived and found cleared fields ready for cultivation.

=== p-00502 (1635) ===
The 1665 version of the “Black Death” plague began in London in the poor, overcrowded parish of St. Giles-in-the-Field.  It started slowly at first and by May of 1665, 43 had died.  In June 6,137 more people died, in July 17,036 people died and at its peak in August, 31,159 people died.  In all, 15% of the English population perished during that terrible 1665 summer.  Incubation period took a mere four to six days and when the plague appeared in a household, the house was sealed, thus condemning the whole family to death!  These houses were distinguished by a painted red cross on the door and the words, “Lord have mercy on us”.  At night the dead corpses were brought out in answer to the cry,” Bring out your dead” and put in carts and taken away to the plague earth dugout pits.  The plague spread to most areas in England.  The city of York was badly affected.  King Charles II and his Court staff fled London and moved out to Oxford to avoid the plague.

=== p-00529 (1636) ===
The major and decisive battle of the King Philip’s War occurred in the “Battle at the Great Swamp Fort”.  On 19 Dec 1675, hoping to prevent a spring Indian onslaught, Plymouth Colony’s Governor Josiah Winslow gathered the Army of the United Colonies and attacked a massive Narragansett and Wampanoag village fortification near the Great Swamp in West Kingston in the Rhode Island Colony.  The following account of the battle appeared in the New England Historical and Genealogical Register Jan 1886.

=== p-00530 (1636) ===
“About one o’clock in the afternoon on the 19 Dec 1675 the Army of the United Colonies came upon an Indian fortress built on a small island in the midst of a large cedar swamp.  Access to the fort was restricted to a strongly defended single-file path through the swamp land.  The severer cold of the previous day and night had frozen the muddy surface of swamp land near the fortified island allowing the colonial soldiers to use temporary alternate paths to approach the fortified camp.  The fort was surrounded by water and a clay wall covered with felled trees.  The barrier was impressive, but was not finished.  An unfinished opening in the wall provided a weakness in the defense the Colonial Army took advantage of in their attack.  If the wall had been finished and the ground was not frozen an attack on the fort would have been suicide.”

=== p-00531 (1636) ===
“Three of the companies of the Massachusetts Militia, including the Captain Johnson’s company with Lt. Phineas Upham, were the first to attack the Native fort.  Two Company Commanders, Captain Johnson and Captain Davenport were both killed at the start of the attack.  The Native warriors had constructed a fortified blockhouse near the opening in the wall that was attack by the colonial militia soldiers and the Native warriors were able to lay down a fury of musket fire on the militia soldiers that killed the two captains, many of their soldiers and wounded many others.  The colonial soldiers were force to fall back and stop the attack.  Lt. Upham took over command of Captain’s Johnson’s company.  The Massachusetts Regiment Commanded Major Appleton arrived with the remainder of the colonial soldiers that had just completed a thirty-four-mile march just as the initial attack fallback had been completed.  The larger colonial force regrouped and attacked the Native fort a second time.  The second charge of the colonial soldiers was able push inside the fort wall and take out the Native warriors that were firing from the fortified blockhouse.  The Plymouth soldiers soon joined in the fighting and after three hours of fighting the fort was set ablaze the Native warrior resistance finally stopped and many of the remaining Narragansett people fled into the swamp.  In the second attack Lt. Upham was wounded.  The exact number of Native warriors and other Narragansetts people in the fort that were killed is unknown, but was estimated to be about three hundred Native warriors and many other Narragansetts people were killed in the fires that destroyed the Native fortified village.”

The Puritans in the colonies worried that their religion and themselves were once again under attack.

Note:  Around 1712, the “Industrial Revolution” started in Great Britain.  At this time most people Britain resided in small, rural communities where their daily existences revolved around farming.  Life was difficult, incomes were meager and malnourishment and disease were common.  People produced the bulk of their own food, clothing, furniture and tools.  Manufacturing was done in homes or small, rural shops using hand tools or simple machines. === p-00665 (1647) ===
In 1763, George Grenville became the new British Prime Minister.  Great Britain had just won the “Seven Year War” and the “French and Indian War” against the French.  The victories were great, but Great Britain was in a desperate need of money to pay off its large war debts and the ongoing in the cost of the maintaining 10,000 British troops in North America.  The primary reason for retaining such a large British military force in North America after the war was to prevent the demobilizing the army and putting some 1,500 loyal British military officers out of work.  It became politically prudent to retain a large peacetime British army, but citizens back in Great Britain were averse to maintaining a standing army at home, so it was necessary to leave and garrison of troops in North America.  Raising taxes in Britain was also out of the question, since there had been virulent (strong) protests when a tax on the production of cider had been proposed.  Another source of money was need to pay the British army garrison in North America.

The proclamation and its probation of access to western lands was one of the first significant areas of dispute between Britain and its American Colonies and will become a contributing factor leading to the later American Revolution. American Revolution

=== p-00673 (1648) ===
On 5 Mar 1770, the “Boston Massacre” occurred in the City of Boston in the Province of Massachusetts Bay.  The massacre occurred when a group of nine British soldiers shot five American colonists in a crowd of three to four hundred colonists who were harassing the soldiers verbally and throwing various projectiles at the solders.  The event was heavily publicized as "a massacre" by leading American Patriots.  British troops had been stationed in the Province of Massachusetts Bay since 1768 in order to support the British crown-appointed officials and to enforce unpopular British Parliamentary legislation.

=== p-00674 (1648) ===
Three years later on 16 Dec 1773, the “Boston Tea Party” occurred as a political protest at Griffin's Wharf in the city of Boston, in the Province of Massachusetts.  The American colonists dressed as Native American warriors demonstrated their frustration and angry at Britain for imposing a new tax on tea and dumped 342 chests of tea into the Boston harbor.

=== p-00675 (1648) ===
On 20 May 1774, the British Parliament passed the “Massachusetts Government Act” that revoked the 1691 charter of the Province of Massachusetts Bay and appointed a new British Royal governor over the former province with wide-ranging powers.  The Massachusetts colonial leaders argued the act altered the basic historical structure of American colonial government and vehemently opposed the new act and would not let it operate.

=== p-00676 (1648) ===
In Oct 1774, the “Province of Massachusetts Bay” came to an end, when members of the General Court of Massachusetts met in violation of the “Massachusetts Government Act” and established the “Massachusetts Provincial Congress”.  Although the new British Governor Gage continued military rule in Boston, the new provincial congress had in effect rule over the rest of the former province.  Hostilities between the British troops and rebel militia colonists six months later started the American Revolutionary in April 1775 at the towns of Lexington and Concord, and continued with the siege of the City of Boston.

=== p-00677 (1649) ===
On 17 Mar 1776, the British were forced to evacuate the City of Boston, ending the siege of the city and bringing the city under colonial rebel control.  On May 1, 1776, the provincial congress adopted a resolution declaring the Province of Massachusetts to be independent of the crown; this was followed up on 4 Jul 1776 by the “Declaration of Independence”, declaring the independence of all thirteen of the American Colonies.  In Oct 1779, a new Constitution of the Commonwealth of Massachusetts was agreed upon and went into effect in Jun 1780.  In elections held in in Oct 1780, John Hancock was elected the first Governor of the State of Massachusetts along with representatives to the commonwealth's first General Court.

Colonel Timothy Bedel and Lieutenant Issacs Butterfield, leaders of the American Continental force at the Cedars, were both court martialed (a military trial) and cashiered (a ritual dismissal from the Continental Army) for their roles in the Cedars garrison surrender.

=== p-01664 (1637) ===
French and Indian War

=== p-01665 (1637) ===
On 28 May 1754, a war started when a British Colonial Militia unit led by a 22-year-old Lt. Colonel George Washington ambushed a small French force in the “Battle of Jumonville Glen”.  The French reacted to the attack on their soldiers and the “French and Indian War” started.  The war in North America was between the France’s Canadian Provinces and their native Indian allies against Great Britain and its North American colonies.  Both the French and English sent large armies from Europe to fight in North America.  One of the first results of the war occurred when the French Acadians living in Nova Scotia refused to swear allegiance to the British king and were expelled from their homes.  The French Acadians living in Nova Scotia were forced to scatter throughout the North American.  Some of the Acadian (pronounced ‘a-cay-DJYEN in French) settled in the French Louisiana colony.  The word “Cajun” derives from the word “Acadian”.

=== p-01670 (1637) ===
The conflict quickly exploded across the British and French colonies in North America and extended to the seizure of hundreds of French merchant ships at sea by the British Navy.  The war soon changed to a European style fight with large armies facing each other in the Canadian heartland.  The British placed William Pitt, who was the leader of the House of Commons, in charge of British war effort.  The British forces included 20,000 regular British soldiers and 22,000 colonial militia troops.  The British soon captured Fort Duquesne in the Ohio River Valley.  The Iroquois Confederation perceiving a shift in favor of the British and ended their neutral position in the war and joined with the British.  The Iroquois involvement quickly resulted in a series of loses of the French forts in the Iroquois areas.

=== p-01685 (1638) ===
In 1774, the “Québec Act of 1774” was enacted by Great Britain’s Parliament setting up procedures for the governance in the “Province of Québec”.  The Act officially recognized: French civil laws in British Québec Province, Roman Catholicism as the official religion in Québec and French as the official language in Québec.  Among the other components of the “Québec Act” were the restoration of the use of the French civil law for private matters, while maintaining the use of the English common law for public administration (including criminal prosecution), rewording  of the oath of allegiance in the Québec Province so that it no longer made reference to the Protestant faith, and guaranteed free practice of the Catholic faith.  The Act's principal purpose was to prevent French Québec from joining the 13 American Colonies in their rebellion against British rule.  The fact that the “Québec Act” granted the French colonists some independence and control over civil matters helped incited resistance to British rule in the American colonies, which observe the obvious differences between the way the French Canadians and the loyal British Americans colonists were being treated.

=== p-01687 (1639) ===
In 1775, the “American Revolutionary War” broke out and British Canada became a target for American forces, that sought to liberate the French population from British rule. The two armies joined forces, but were defeated in the “Battle of Québec”, in which the American Continental General Richard Montgomery was killed.  The Continental Army was driven back out of Canada and back into New York by the arrival of a large army of British soldiers and mercenary hired German soldiers in June 1776.

=== p-01808 (1647) ===
Dakota Territory

=== p-01809 (1647) ===
The “Dakota Territory” consisted of the northernmost part of the land acquired back in the 1803 in the Louisiana Purchase from France and the later 1818 agreement with British Canada that established the border between Canada and the United States at the 49th parallel.  The name “Dakota” refers to the Dakota branch of the Native American Sioux Nation people who were living in the area at the time.  Most of new “Dakota Territory” was formerly part of the Minnesota and Nebraska territories.

=== p-01810 (1647) ===
When Minnesota became a state in 1858, the leftover area between the Missouri River and the and Minnesota's western boundary was left unorganized, from the United States government’s perspective but not the perspective of the Sioux Nation.  In 1858, the Yankton Sioux Native American tribe ceded (gave up) most of its eastern South Dakota land to the United States.  The early settlers that were living in the area formed a provisional government, but failed to received United States territory status.  In 1861, President-elect Abraham Lincoln’s cousin-in-law John Blair Smith Todd lobbied for territory status for the area and the U.S. Congress formally created “Dakota Territory” on 2 Mar 1861.  President Lincoln appointed his old friend and neighbor from Springfield, Illinois William Jayne the first Dakota Territory governor.  See on the map below the 1861 “Dakota Territory”:

=== p-01811 (1647) ===
On 20 May 1862 President Abraham Lincoln signed the “Homestead Act”.  The act granted the U. S. Federal Government more than 270 million acres of land for homesteading.  To make a homestead claim, a potential homesteader had to pay a filing fee of $10 to make a temporary claim on the land, plus $2 for commission to the land agent and an additional $6 final payment to receive an official patent on the land.  Land titles could also be purchased from the government for $1.25 per acre following six months proven residency.  See maps below showing the lands that were taken from the Native American people and was opened to settlers:

=== p-01812 (1647) ===
As new lands became available in Iowa, Minnesota and Wisconsin the 1850s, they were quickly claimed by new Euro-American settlers that initially used the land for “subsistence farming”, the growing and raising the food needed to allow the family to survive.  By 1860 most of the land in the three states was all claimed.  The American Civil War disrupted the “subsistence farming” system as men were called up to serve in the U.S. Army.  Between 1850 and 1860 new railroad lines were constructed in the three states that change the farming process from primarily one of feeding the farm family to “commodity farming” were farm products were grown and raised to be sold for a profit.  How well the settlers’ families adopted the new entrepreneurial farming opportunity resulted in some successful farms growing in size and the less successful farming families’ fathers and sons having to work on the more successful farms as farmworkers for wages.

=== p-01813 (1647) ===
The change from subsistence farming to commodity farming required access to money.  Growing crops and raising animals exceeding the farm family’s own minimal needs required the planting of larger areas of farm fields and raising more farm animals to sell, which each required up-front money to pay the costs of seeds, farm animals and salaries of farmworkers before money was made from selling the farm’s products.  The money problems were made worse because most new farming family had no money.  If fact most were already in debt after paying their relocation costs and $24.25 for five acres of homestead farmland and each additional acre of land cost $1.25 more.

=== p-01814 (1647) ===
In 1862, to help the United States fund the Civil War the U.S. Congress passed the “Legal Tender Act”, authorizing the printing of $150 million in new U.S. paper money, colored in distinctive green color on the backside, which was quickly nicknamed  the “Greenback”.  The “Legal Tender Act” was followed up by Congress creating a National Banking System, which was  authorized to issue banknotes backed by the U.S. Government.  Government Reserve Banks were next formed in the country’s largest cities holding money reserves to backup local banks established in the country’s smaller towns.  With a new banking system in place settler families were able now to borrow money to buy homestead farming property.  If the settler farmer family was unable to grow or raise extra farming products they could sell, there was little chance they could pay off their borrowed bank loan.

=== p-01864 (1651) ===
The unincorporated farm community of Arvilla in the North Dakota Territory was names after Arvilla Estella Hersey the wife of Dudley Hersey. The Hersey Farm raised thoroughbred Clydesdale workhorses and high-class Jersey milk cows.  There were also 100 hogs; and several hundred Plymouth Rock hens, all of prime stock.  Dudley Hersey also established a townsite near his large farm and names it after  his wife Arvilla.  Dudley Hersey died in 1900 and his wife Arvilla moved away and the magnificence farm quickly faded away.

=== p-01905 (1651) ===
Great Depression and Dust Bowl

=== p-01907 (1653) ===
As a rural farming state, North Dakota suffered greatly when the prices received for farm produce declined.  The search for a solution brought about different movements in the 1920s and 1930s.  Farmers banded together to market their produce and reduce the costs of farming.  A “North Dakota Farmers Union” was formed and quickly spread across the state’s prairies.  The local Farmers Unions, built grain elevators and organized petroleum cooperatives that served the needs of an increasingly mechanized rural farming economy.  In 1932, the cooperative group helped form a militant defense organization the “Farmers Holiday Organization”, to take direct action against low commodity prices and farm foreclosures.

=== p-01908 (1653) ===
The militancy in rural North Dakota farm areas quickly spread into state politics.  A revitalized Nonpartisan League (NPL) emerged in 1932, electing the colorful populist William Langer as the state’s Governor.  William Langer took bold actions when he assumed his Governor’s office in 1933; he slashed state spending, imposed moratoriums on mortgage foreclosure sales, and embargoed shipment of grain from the state.  However, his disregard of law brought Federal investigations, and in 1934 he was convicted of campaign law violations and removed from office; Lt. Governor Ole Olson finished the governor term.  That same year, a divided NPL lost the Governor's election to Democrat Thomas Moodie, who assumed office in 1935, but was quickly disqualified when an investigation discovered that he did not meet state residency requirements.  Lt. Governor Walter Welford succeeded ousted Thomas Moodie, becoming the fourth Governor in seven months.

=== p-01909 (1653) ===
Despite economic problems, crop failures, dust storms, and weather extremes, North Dakota visibly modernized during the 1930s.  Government spending increase on projects that built a new skyscraper state capitol building, improved the state’s highways and roads, created state parks, and expanded city services throughout the state.

=== p-01910 (1653) ===
The Great Depression of the 1930s both slowed progress and sped change.  Heavy farm debt and low farm commodity prices caused farm foreclosures and bank failures.  Successful farmers who had better handle their financial situation, were able enlarged their farm holdings by buying foreclosed farm property.  The rural population in the state diminished while the state’s cities grew.  Thousands of North Dakotans lost their farms and were forced either moved into the cities and towns or out of the state altogether.  It has been estimated that over 70% of the state's people required one form of public assistance during the period.

=== p-01916 (1653) ===
Note:  The Great Depression affected almost everyone living in North Dakota.  The practice of taking in roomers (renters) in one’s home help cover one’s living costs was common and help both the renter and the roomer survive the desperate times.
